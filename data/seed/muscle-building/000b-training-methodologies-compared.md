# Training methodologies compared — pick the split that fits your life

There is no single "best" split. The right one is the one that lets you hit
adequate volume per muscle, recover, and stay consistent for months. Below are
the most common programming structures, who they suit, and the trade-offs.

## Full-body, 3× per week

- **Structure:** every training day hits every major muscle. Usually one
  compound lift per movement pattern (squat, hinge, push, pull) plus a couple
  of isolation lifts.
- **Frequency per muscle:** 3× per week.
- **Volume per muscle per session:** low to moderate (3–5 sets).
- **Best for:** beginners, busy people who can only train 3 days, anyone
  returning from a layoff, athletes whose primary sport isn't lifting.
- **Why it works:** highest practical frequency means every session refreshes
  the muscle-protein-synthesis spike — small, frequent stimuli compound fast.
  Easy to recover from individual sessions because per-muscle volume is low.
- **Limitations:** at intermediate-and-up training age you may want more
  per-muscle volume than 3× per week can comfortably fit in a 60-minute
  session.

## Upper / lower, 4× per week

- **Structure:** two upper-body and two lower-body sessions per week.
- **Frequency per muscle:** 2× per week.
- **Volume per muscle per session:** moderate (5–8 sets).
- **Best for:** intermediates, anyone who can commit to 4 sessions but not
  more, people who like balanced strength + size emphasis.
- **Why it works:** classic middle ground. Each muscle gets hit hard enough
  to drive growth and trained often enough to keep the protein synthesis
  signal alive. Sessions are ~60 minutes each.
- **Limitations:** big upper days can get crowded (chest + back + delts +
  arms in one session). Cutting volume on smaller muscles is often the
  necessary trade-off.

## Push / Pull / Legs (PPL), 6× per week

- **Structure:** push (chest, delts, triceps), pull (back, biceps, rear delts),
  legs (quads, hamstrings, glutes, calves). Usually run twice through per week.
- **Frequency per muscle:** 2× per week.
- **Volume per muscle per session:** high (8–12 sets).
- **Best for:** intermediate-to-advanced lifters with 5–6 days of gym
  availability, people who want to specialise in physique work.
- **Why it works:** lets you accumulate high weekly volume per muscle (up to
  ~20+ sets) by spreading it over two relatively long sessions. Each muscle
  gets one "specialise" day per cycle.
- **Limitations:** legs-day frequency of only 2× per week is fine, but
  rest-day allocation matters. 6 days a week is also a lot of stress on
  recovery when life gets busy.

## The classic bro split, 5× per week

- **Structure:** chest day, back day, shoulder day, arm day, leg day.
- **Frequency per muscle:** 1× per week.
- **Volume per muscle per session:** very high (10–20 sets, often more).
- **Best for:** advanced bodybuilders (especially enhanced lifters who have
  the recovery to handle it) and people who just enjoy the focused-session
  feel. NOT recommended for beginners or natural intermediates.
- **Why it works:** the volume per session is extreme. For people with
  strong recovery and a high training age, it can drive specialisation.
- **Limitations:** once-a-week frequency means each muscle's protein
  synthesis spike only fires once. Modern evidence consistently shows
  2× / week beats 1× / week for natural lifters at equated volume. Most
  people running a "bro split" would build more muscle on PPL or U/L.

## Conjugate / Westside

- **Structure:** rotating max-effort and dynamic-effort days, lots of
  variation of the main lift week to week. Mostly a powerlifting method.
- **Best for:** competitive powerlifters or strength athletes. Not optimal
  for pure hypertrophy goals — too much neural fatigue, not enough
  consistent repetition of growth-driving movements.

## How to choose

Run through this short decision tree:

1. **How many days per week can you realistically train, every week, for
   3 months?** Be honest. The best program is the one you actually do.
   - 3 days → full-body, 3× per week.
   - 4 days → upper/lower or push/pull-legs-rest-push/pull.
   - 5–6 days → push/pull/legs (×2), or upper/lower + a specialisation day.

2. **What is your training age?**
   - <1 year of consistent training → full-body or upper/lower. You'll grow
     on almost anything. Don't optimise prematurely.
   - 1–3 years → upper/lower or PPL. Now per-muscle volume matters more.
   - 3+ years → whatever supports the per-muscle volume you've identified
     drives the best growth for you. Periodise — change emphasis every few
     months.

3. **How recoverable are you?** Bad sleep, high job stress, poor appetite,
   chronic injuries → fewer total sessions, more rest days. A "high-frequency"
   program done while exhausted is a high-frequency way to plateau.

## Volume guidelines per muscle group (natural lifters)

These are starting points. Adjust based on what produces measurable progress
on the lift (more reps at the same weight, more weight at the same reps).

| Muscle | Sets per week (low–high) |
|---|---|
| Quads | 10–18 |
| Hamstrings | 8–14 |
| Glutes | 10–16 |
| Chest | 10–18 |
| Back | 12–20 |
| Side delts | 12–20 (high tolerance for volume) |
| Biceps | 8–16 |
| Triceps | 8–16 |
| Calves | 8–16 |
| Abs | 8–16 |

## Progressive overload — the only thing that matters long-term

Whatever split you pick, you need to add load or reps to the key compound
lifts over time. The simplest method:

1. Pick a key lift per movement pattern (squat, hinge, push, pull).
2. Each session, try to add one rep at the same weight, OR add the smallest
   plate available at the same reps.
3. When you can't progress for 2–3 sessions in a row, deload (drop volume by
   ~40% for a week), then resume.
4. Every 8–12 weeks, switch some accessory lifts to maintain joint health
   and break up plateaus.

A split that lets you progressively overload for 6+ months is doing its job.
One that constantly stalls you, regardless of how trendy it is, is not.
