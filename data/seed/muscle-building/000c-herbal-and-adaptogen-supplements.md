# Herbal & adaptogen supplements — what's worth it for muscle building

Herbal supplements are aggressively marketed to lifters and the evidence is
much weaker than the marketing suggests. Below is each major herbal / botanical
sold for hypertrophy, testosterone, or recovery, with the dose, monthly cost,
expected timeframe, evidence level, and a verdict.

The pattern you'll notice: most herbs that DO have some effect work via
peripheral mechanisms (better sleep, lower cortisol) rather than directly
making muscle grow. That's still useful — sleep and stress matter — but it's
not "natural anabolic" magic.

## Ashwagandha (Withania somnifera)

- **Why people take it:** stress / cortisol reduction; some studies show
  modest strength and lean mass gains in untrained populations.
- **Dose:** 300–600 mg/day of a standardised extract (KSM-66 or Sensoril).
- **Monthly cost:** ~$15–25.
- **Best for:** lifters with high stress / poor sleep, beginners. Less useful
  for already-recovered trained lifters.
- **Time to effect:** sleep / mood effects in 2–4 weeks; the small strength /
  size signal (where it exists) is over 8–12 weeks.
- **Evidence level:** MODERATE for stress / sleep / mild cortisol reduction.
  WEAK-to-MODERATE for direct lean-mass gain — the studies are mostly small,
  in untrained or specifically-stressed populations, with varying extracts.
- **Verdict:** worth trying if your stress / sleep is poor. Don't expect a
  dramatic body composition shift on top of an already-dialled program.
- **Caveats:** can interact with thyroid medication, immunosuppressants, and
  sedatives. Pregnant or autoimmune-condition patients should talk to a doctor.

## Tongkat ali (Eurycoma longifolia)

- **Why people take it:** small testosterone increases (~10–15%) in some
  studies, primarily in men with low baseline testosterone or under stress.
- **Dose:** 200–400 mg/day of a standardised extract.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$25–40.
- **Best for:** men with documented low-normal testosterone; older lifters.
- **Time to effect:** 4–12 weeks for any hormonal shift.
- **Evidence level:** WEAK-to-MODERATE. The data exists but the studies are
  small and the effect size for muscle growth specifically is unproven.
  Most published lean-mass numbers are tiny or inconsistent.
- **Verdict:** the boring answer applies — if your sleep, training, food,
  and creatine are all dialled, then tongkat ali might be a small extra. If
  any of those are missing, fix them first.

## Turmeric / curcumin

- **Why people take it:** anti-inflammatory effects, joint pain relief, possible
  reduction in delayed-onset muscle soreness.
- **Dose:** 500–1000 mg curcumin (with piperine or a liposomal / phytosomal
  form for absorption).
- **Monthly cost:** ~$15–25 for a quality formulation.
- **Best for:** lifters with chronic joint discomfort or post-workout soreness
  that affects training frequency.
- **Time to effect:** 2–4 weeks for joint / inflammation effects.
- **Evidence level:** MODERATE for inflammation and joint pain markers. NONE
  for direct muscle building.
- **Verdict:** a recovery supplement, not a growth supplement. Reasonable
  for older lifters or those with cranky joints.

## Ginseng (Panax ginseng / American ginseng)

- **Why people take it:** energy, cognitive, possible fatigue resistance.
- **Dose:** 200–400 mg/day of standardised extract.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$20–35.
- **Best for:** lifters who are chronically fatigued and want a non-stimulant
  energy nudge. Caffeine usually does more for less.
- **Evidence level:** WEAK for direct training adaptations. MODERATE for
  subjective energy / cognition in fatigued populations.
- **Verdict:** skip — money better spent on caffeine and sleep hygiene.

## Fadogia agrestis

- **Why people take it:** marketed as a testosterone booster, popularised
  by podcasters in the last few years.
- **Dose:** the marketed doses vary wildly; the studies that exist are in
  rats, at doses that aren't directly translatable to humans.
- **Evidence level:** **VERY WEAK in humans.** There are no quality RCTs
  showing muscle or testosterone benefit in humans. Safety profile in
  humans is poorly characterised; some animal data shows liver and kidney
  toxicity at high doses.
- **Verdict:** skip. The signal is "podcaster recommended this" not "the
  evidence supports this." If it ever gets a proper trial showing
  meaningful benefit and acceptable safety, that changes.

## Maca (Lepidium meyenii)

- **Why people take it:** energy, mood, libido. Sometimes marketed for
  hypertrophy.
- **Dose:** 1.5–3 g/day of root powder.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$10–20.
- **Evidence level:** WEAK for energy / libido in some populations. NONE
  for muscle building specifically.
- **Verdict:** a libido / mood supplement at best, not a hypertrophy aid.

## Rhodiola rosea

- **Why people take it:** stress adaptation, fatigue resistance,
  cognitive performance under stress.
- **Dose:** 200–600 mg/day of standardised extract (3% rosavins / 1%
  salidroside).
- **Monthly cost:** ~$15–25.
- **Best for:** lifters experiencing burnout / chronic fatigue.
- **Evidence level:** MODERATE for fatigue resistance and mental
  performance under stress. WEAK-to-NONE for direct training adaptations.
- **Verdict:** useful for a specific population (burned-out, fatigued),
  not as a generic muscle-building add-on.

## Tribulus terrestris

- **Why people take it:** historically marketed as a testosterone booster.
- **Evidence level:** **NONE.** Multiple well-controlled studies have shown
  no testosterone increase and no muscle / strength benefit in healthy men.
- **Verdict:** skip. This was a hopeful idea two decades ago and the data
  killed it. It persists in product blends because it's cheap and the
  marketing inertia is high.

## Fenugreek

- **Why people take it:** weak testosterone-boosting claims, libido.
- **Evidence level:** WEAK for libido in some studies. WEAK-to-NONE for
  muscle / strength.
- **Verdict:** skip for muscle building purposes.

## D-aspartic acid (DAA)

- **Why people take it:** earliest studies suggested testosterone increases.
- **Evidence level:** the follow-up trials have mostly NOT replicated the
  initial signal. Some show testosterone *decreases* with prolonged use.
- **Verdict:** skip. Initial promise didn't hold up.

## Cordyceps (mushroom extract)

- **Why people take it:** endurance, VO2max claims.
- **Dose:** 1–3 g/day.
- **Evidence level:** WEAK for endurance / VO2max in trained populations.
  NONE for hypertrophy.
- **Verdict:** an endurance supplement at best, not relevant for hypertrophy.

## Reishi, lion's mane, chaga (mushroom adaptogens)

- These are general-wellness supplements. No meaningful hypertrophy evidence.
  Lion's mane has some cognitive evidence. Skip for muscle-building goals.

## Tier list for muscle-building goals

Restricted to herbals / adaptogens:

- **Reasonable to try (specific situations):** ashwagandha (stressed / sleep
  issues), turmeric (joint pain), rhodiola (burnout / fatigue), tongkat ali
  (older lifters with low-normal T).
- **Don't bother:** Tribulus, fenugreek, D-aspartic acid, maca for muscle,
  ginseng for muscle.
- **Avoid until better data:** Fadogia agrestis — safety profile in humans
  is unclear, efficacy data is weak.

## How to evaluate a herbal supplement claim

When a new herb gets hyped, ask:

1. **Are the studies in humans?** Rat data does not reliably translate.
2. **Are the studies in trained or untrained subjects?** Beginners respond
   to almost everything; the meaningful question is whether trained lifters
   show an effect.
3. **What is the effect size?** "Statistically significant" can mean a 2%
   difference. That's not a body-composition supplement.
4. **Who funded the study?** Manufacturer-funded studies are not invalid but
   warrant a higher bar of independent replication.
5. **Is the active ingredient standardised?** Most herbal extracts vary
   wildly between brands; non-standardised products may contain little of
   the supposed active compound.

The boring truth: across every herbal supplement marketed to lifters, the
expected effect on muscle growth is at most a small fraction of what creatine,
protein adequacy, sleep, and well-programmed training already deliver. Spend
the time and money on those first.
