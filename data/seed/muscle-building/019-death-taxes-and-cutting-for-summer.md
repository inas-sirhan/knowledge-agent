Death, taxes, and cutting for summer

There are a few things in life you can count on.

Death.
Taxes.
And every year, like clockwork, a wave of people decides in late spring that now is the time to get lean for summer.

It’s predictable. It’s understandable. And it’s also why most people either don’t get as lean as they want… or hate the entire process by the time July rolls around.

Here’s the truth. The people who look the best in summer didn’t start in summer. They didn’t wait for motivation, panic, or a calendar reminder. They started early, stayed consistent, and built momentum before anyone else even realized they needed to.

If you’re reading this around tax day, you’re not late. But you’re right on the edge of becoming late.
Let’s talk about how to do this the right way.

Why starting early wins every time

The anti-panic diet

Fat loss takes longer than most people want to admit.
Not Instagram longer. Not “12 weeks shredded” longer. Real life longer.

A sustainable rate of fat loss is usually somewhere in the range of about 0.5 to 1 percent of body weight per week. That’s not sexy. It doesn’t sell. But it works.

When you start early, you give yourself room to:

- Diet at a moderate pace

- Keep training performance high

- Preserve muscle

- Avoid feeling like a zombie

When you start late, everything gets compressed. The deficit gets more aggressive. Energy drops faster. Training suffers. Adherence becomes a grind instead of a routine.

Picture two people:

One starts in April. They run a moderate deficit, train hard, keep protein high, and chip away week by week. By summer, they’re lean, still performing well, and not completely burned out.

The other starts in late May. They slash calories, double their cardio, and try to force results. By June, they’re tired, flat, and questioning all their life choices.

Both “dieted.” Only one did it in a way that actually works.

You are not behind in April. But if you wait until May, you’re going to feel it.

What a smart summer cut actually looks like

Spoiler: it’s kind of boring

A good fat loss phase doesn’t feel like a crash diet.
It doesn’t feel like punishment. It doesn’t require perfect execution. And it definitely doesn’t rely on extremes.

At its core, a smart cut is simple:

- A moderate calorie deficit

- High protein intake

- Consistent resistance training

- Cardio and steps used strategically, not emotionally

That’s it.

No weird macro splits like 331 carbs, 212 protein, 57 fat. No chasing precision that doesn’t actually exist. Your body doesn’t care if you hit your macros to the gram. It responds to trends over time.

Calories and protein drive most of your results. Everything else is refinement.

The goal is not to suffer as much as possible. The goal is to create something repeatable. Something you can execute on a random Tuesday when work is busy, your kids are wild, and your motivation is nonexistent.

Because those are the days that actually determine your outcome.

Structure without the handcuffs

How to diet like a normal human

One of the biggest mistakes people make when dieting is swinging too far in either direction.
They either go full chaos with no structure, or they try to follow a plan so rigid it breaks the moment real life shows up.

The sweet spot is in the middle.
Structure gives you a framework:

- Planned meals

- Consistent protein intake

- A general daily target

Flexibility makes it sustainable:

- Adjusting meals when plans change

- Shifting calories across the day

- Letting the week matter more than a single day

Your body doesn’t reset at midnight. It responds to the average of what you do over time.

That means if you have a higher calorie day on Saturday, you’re not “off track.” You’re just… living your life. As long as the week is controlled, you’re still progressing.

The best diet is not the one that works perfectly Monday through Friday. It’s the one that still works on Saturday.

Navigating summer without blowing it

BBQs, vacations, and not losing your mind

Summer is not the problem.

BBQs, vacations, pool days, and social events are not the reason people lose progress. The problem is how people approach them.

Most people treat these situations like all-or-nothing events. They either try to be perfect and miserable, or they completely abandon the plan and tell themselves they’ll “restart Monday.”

Neither works.

A better approach looks like this:

- Eat a solid protein-focused meal earlier in the day

- Save calories for the event instead of pretending it’s not happening

- Make reasonable choices without trying to be perfect

- Accept that some days will be higher and move on

You don’t need to out-cardio a burger. You don’t need to skip every fun food to stay lean. You need to control the week, not dominate a single meal.

Consistency beats perfection every time.

The most overlooked phase: staying lean

Where most people mess it up

Getting lean is only half the battle.
Staying lean is where most people fail.
The common pattern looks like this:

- Diet hard

- Get lean

- Immediately relax everything

- Gain back a significant amount of weight

Not because they’re lazy. Because they never transitioned out of the diet properly.
Maintenance is not a failure phase. It’s a skill.
After a cut, the goal should be to:

- Gradually increase calories

- Stabilize body weight

- Push training performance

- Let your body recover

This is where you actually start to look your best. Fuller, stronger, and more energized.
It’s also what allows you to enjoy summer without feeling like you’re one meal away from undoing everything.

If you can’t maintain it, you didn’t really achieve it.

What if you’re late?

The honest answer

If you’re reading this and thinking, “Yeah, I probably should have started earlier,” you’re not alone.
And you’re not screwed.

You can still make meaningful progress. You just need to adjust your expectations and your approach.

That might mean:

- Running a slightly more aggressive deficit

- Tightening up your consistency

- Accepting that “better” is the goal, not perfect

You may not get stage lean. But you can absolutely look noticeably leaner, feel better, and build momentum heading into the rest of the year.

Late is not ruined. Late just requires discipline and a little humility.

The real third certainty
Death.
Taxes.
And people starting their summer cut too late.
You can be part of that cycle. Or you can break it.

Start now.
Keep it simple.
Stay consistent when it’s boring and when it’s inconvenient.
The goal isn’t to suffer your way into summer.
It’s to show up already ready.
