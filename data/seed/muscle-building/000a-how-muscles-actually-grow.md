# How muscles actually grow — a working primer

This is the mechanistic story underneath every training recommendation. If you
understand these levers, every disagreement about "the best program" becomes
easier to reason about, because most of them are arguments over which mechanism
to optimise.

## The three classical drivers of hypertrophy

For about twenty years the dominant framework (popularised by Brad Schoenfeld)
has been that three signals drive muscle growth:

1. **Mechanical tension** — the force a muscle fibre actually has to produce.
   Heavy loads OR lighter loads taken close to failure both create high tension
   in the recruited fibres. This is the dominant driver. Most contemporary
   evidence says mechanical tension explains the bulk of long-term hypertrophy
   and the other two signals are downstream of it.
2. **Metabolic stress** — the burn of accumulating metabolites (lactate, H+,
   inorganic phosphate) during high-rep work with short rest. It contributes,
   but probably less than people thought a decade ago.
3. **Muscle damage** — micro-tears in the fibre from novel or eccentric load.
   It used to be sold as "no soreness, no growth," but the modern view is that
   damage is a side-effect of mechanical tension, not an independent driver.
   Excess damage is just extra recovery cost.

Practical translation: **lifting weights heavy enough that you can't do many
more reps, for enough total sets, over enough weeks** is the engine. Everything
else is fiddling.

## The molecular signal: mTOR and muscle protein synthesis

When a muscle fibre experiences high tension under load, the cell membrane
deforms, mechanoreceptors trigger a signalling cascade, and within minutes the
**mTOR complex 1 (mTORC1)** ramps up muscle protein synthesis (MPS). MPS stays
elevated for ~24–48 hours after a hypertrophy session.

You can think of every training session as opening a window in which the
muscle is unusually receptive to building new contractile protein. Sleep,
adequate protein (the leucine threshold is ~2.5–3 g per meal, hit it with
~30–40 g of high-quality protein), and a non-deficit caloric state widen that
window. Severe under-eating, poor sleep, and chronic stress narrow it.

## Satellite cells and the long game

For sustained hypertrophy beyond the first few months, **satellite cells**
(muscle stem cells lying dormant outside the fibre) get activated, divide, and
fuse into the existing fibre — donating new nuclei. More nuclei = more
machinery to make new contractile protein = a higher ceiling for growth.

This is why detraining is recoverable: those donated nuclei stick around for
months. It's the molecular basis of "muscle memory" — re-gaining lost mass is
faster than the first time you built it.

## Volume, intensity, frequency: the three knobs you actually turn

Every training plan is a particular combination of three variables.

- **Volume**, usually measured as hard sets per muscle per week. The
  dose-response curve is roughly logarithmic. Most natural lifters see good
  growth in the **10–20 sets per muscle per week** range. Below ~10 sets you
  leave gains on the table; above ~22 sets fatigue accumulates faster than
  growth signal and you slip into junk volume.
- **Intensity**, usually expressed as % of 1RM, or more usefully as
  **proximity to failure** (reps in reserve / RIR). For hypertrophy the
  evidence says the LAST few reps of a set — the ones that feel hard —
  recruit the high-threshold motor units that drive growth. Sets taken to
  0–3 RIR with loads anywhere from ~6 to ~30 reps grow muscle similarly well.
- **Frequency**, how often each muscle gets trained. For a given weekly
  volume, splitting it across **2–3 sessions per muscle per week** is at least
  as effective as one giant session — usually slightly better, and easier on
  joints.

These three trade against each other. Higher per-session volume forces lower
frequency (recovery limits). Higher intensity forces lower volume. Most
intermediate programs sit in the middle of all three knobs because that's where
the math works out.

## What this means in practice

- **The fundamental work is hard sets close to failure on compound lifts,
  loaded progressively over weeks.**
- The "best rep range" debate is mostly a draw. 6–8 reps, 10–15 reps, 20–30
  reps — pick the range that lets you take sets close to failure with good
  form, and rotate periodically.
- More volume helps until it doesn't. Track sets per muscle per week. If
  you're under ~8, add work. If you're over ~22 and not making progress, the
  problem is probably recovery (sleep, food, stress), not "more sets."
- Sleep and protein are not "bonus tips." They are the inputs that turn the
  training stimulus into actual muscle protein. Underfed, under-slept
  lifters are training for fitness, not hypertrophy.

## Common misconceptions

- **"You need to feel sore to grow."** No. Soreness tracks novelty and
  damage, not stimulus. You can grow well with minimal soreness once you're
  adapted to a movement.
- **"High reps build endurance, low reps build size."** Above ~5 reps,
  growth is similar across rep ranges as long as effort is high. Reps below
  5 favour neural strength gains over hypertrophy, but still grow muscle.
- **"You need to confuse your muscles."** Variety helps long-term but the
  primary driver of growth is progressive overload of the same key
  movements. "Muscle confusion" is a marketing term, not a mechanism.
- **"Slow eccentrics are magic."** Tempo matters at the margins. The
  evidence on prolonged 4-second eccentrics shows small effects at best,
  with much higher fatigue cost. Worth using sparingly; not worth building
  a program around.

## A one-paragraph summary you can memorise

Muscles grow because mechanical tension under load activates mTORC1, which
ramps up protein synthesis for a day or two, and over time satellite cells
donate new nuclei that raise the ceiling. To trigger this you need hard sets
close to failure, in the 6–30 rep range, totalling roughly 10–20 sets per
muscle per week, split across 2–3 sessions. To turn that stimulus into actual
new tissue you need adequate protein (~1.6–2.2 g/kg/day), a non-deficit
caloric state when bulking, and 7–9 hours of sleep. Everything else is
optimisation.
