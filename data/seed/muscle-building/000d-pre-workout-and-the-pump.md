# Pre-workout supplements and the pump — what's worth it

This document covers two related topics: **pre-workout formulas** (what's in a
$40 jar and which ingredients earn their place), and **the pump** (does it
matter for growth, and how to maximise it).

## What pre-workout actually does

A "pre-workout" is a blend of ingredients meant to do one or more of:

1. **Increase perceived energy and focus** — almost always via caffeine, often
   plus L-tyrosine or theanine.
2. **Improve work capacity / volume per session** — via citrulline malate
   (nitric-oxide-mediated blood flow), beta-alanine (acidity buffering), and
   sometimes sodium bicarbonate.
3. **Enhance the pump** — via citrulline (NO pathway), glycerol or HydroMax
   (cell hyperhydration), betaine, and (mythologically) agmatine.
4. **Reduce perceived fatigue** — caffeine, tyrosine, taurine, theacrine.

Most of the marketing copy on a pre-workout jar is point 3, but the actual
performance benefit comes mostly from points 1 and 2.

## The ingredients that earn their place

These are the components with reasonable evidence for hypertrophy / training
goals, with effective doses.

### Caffeine — 3-6 mg/kg, 30-60 min pre
The biggest single performance contributor in any pre-workout. Anything below
~150 mg per serving is under-dosed for most lifters. Above ~400 mg is
diminishing returns and starts to compromise sleep if dosed late.

### L-Citrulline (or citrulline malate) — 6-8 g
The pump and work-capacity ingredient with the best evidence. Boosts plasma
arginine more effectively than supplementing arginine directly. Effective dose
is 6-8 g of L-citrulline or 8 g of citrulline malate (which is ~50% citrulline).
Many pre-workouts dose it at 3-4 g — under-dosed.

### Beta-alanine — 3-5 g daily (not per workout)
Works by saturation over weeks, not acutely. Even though it's in pre-workout
blends and produces a tingling "kick-in" feel, it doesn't actually need to be
pre-workout — daily dosing is what matters. The tingling is harmless
paresthesia, not the performance effect.

### L-Tyrosine — 1-2 g
Modest evidence for cognitive performance under stress / fatigue. Cheap to
include, plausibly useful.

### Sodium bicarbonate — 0.2-0.3 g/kg
Buffers blood pH for high-rep / glycolytic work. Good for ~30-90 second efforts.
Causes GI distress in many people — buffered versions exist. Mostly used by
strength athletes for short metcon-style efforts.

### Theanine — 100-200 mg
Pairs well with caffeine. Smooths out the jitter / anxiety without reducing the
focus benefit. Good combo for caffeine-sensitive lifters.

### Creatine — 3-5 g daily
Sometimes included in pre-workouts. Like beta-alanine, the daily total is what
matters; timing doesn't.

## The ingredients that don't earn their place

### Arginine (AAKG)
Outperformed by citrulline for the same nitric-oxide pathway. Cheap filler
ingredient now mostly used for marketing.

### Agmatine sulfate
Marketed as a pump enhancer. Almost no quality human evidence. Avoid paying
for it.

### Beta-hydroxy beta-methylbutyrate (HMB)
Some evidence in detrained / older populations. In trained lifters with
adequate protein, basically no benefit.

### Glycerol / HydroMax
Real pump-enhancer via cell hydration. Works. But the GI tolerance is poor,
and the effect is purely cosmetic / sensory, not a meaningful performance
contributor.

### "Yohimbine" / fat-burner stims
Don't belong in a pre-workout. They're for fasted cardio and they crank up
anxiety and heart rate.

### "Pump matrix" / "Energy matrix" / proprietary blends
A red flag. If a label lists "5,000 mg pump matrix" instead of disclosing
each ingredient dose, you cannot know if the active components are dosed
effectively. Almost always the cheap ingredients dominate and the expensive
ones are at trace levels.

## The mechanism of "the pump" — and does it matter

The pump is a temporary increase in intramuscular blood and fluid volume
during high-rep sets. Mechanically:

1. The contracting muscle compresses local veins → blood flow OUT is reduced.
2. Arterial inflow continues → blood and plasma accumulate in the muscle.
3. Metabolite accumulation (lactate, H+) draws more water into the cell
   (osmotic shift) and dilates local arterioles.
4. The result: the muscle visibly swells.

**Is the pump a growth signal in itself?** Probably, but a relatively small
one. The leading theory (Schoenfeld and others) is that **cell swelling**
under load may sense as a threat-to-cellular-integrity signal that contributes
to MPS activation. The evidence is suggestive but not as strong as the
evidence for mechanical tension.

**Practical translation:** training that produces a strong pump (higher reps,
shorter rest, certain order of exercises) tends to coincide with high
mechanical tension on smaller / isolation muscles, where pump-style work is
useful. But chasing pump for its own sake — without progressive overload — is
chasing a side-effect and missing the main course.

A pump that doesn't accompany progressive overload is just a temporary visual
effect, not a growth event.

## How to maximise the pump (if you want to)

For the sensation and the small possible signal:

- **High reps** (12-20+) on the target muscle.
- **Short rest** (30-90 seconds between sets).
- **Drop sets and rest-pause sets** at the end of a session.
- **Order**: do compound work for the muscle first, then isolation /
  pump-focused work last when the muscle is pre-fatigued.
- **Hydration**: dehydration kills the pump. Drink water through the day
  (don't chug pre-workout — feels sloshy).
- **Carbs in the prior 2-4 hours**: glycogen-stocked muscles hold more water.
- **Citrulline malate 6-8 g**, 30-60 min pre.
- **Optionally**: 1-3 g of sodium and 3-5 g of glycerol if you want the
  extreme pump — accept the GI risk.

## Stim vs non-stim pre-workout

- **Stim** = caffeine + supporting cognitive ingredients. Reach for it when
  you need a hard, focused session and you didn't drink coffee.
- **Non-stim** = pump and work-capacity ingredients only (citrulline,
  beta-alanine, betaine, etc.). Reach for it when:
  - You're training in the evening and don't want sleep impact.
  - You already had caffeine from coffee that day.
  - You're cycling off stims to reset caffeine tolerance.

Many serious lifters use **stim 3-4 days a week** and non-stim or nothing on
the rest, to keep caffeine tolerance low.

## DIY pre-workout — the cheapest effective stack

If you want everything that actually works and nothing that doesn't:

| Ingredient | Dose | Approx monthly cost (daily training) |
|---|---|---|
| Caffeine (capsules or strong coffee) | 200-300 mg | $5 |
| L-citrulline | 6-8 g | $15 |
| Beta-alanine | 3-5 g | $10 |
| Creatine (separately, daily) | 3-5 g | $5 |
| Optional: L-theanine | 100-200 mg | $5 |

Total: ~$35-40/month for the effective stack at full dose, vs $40-60 for a
single jar of a commercial pre-workout that often under-doses several of these.

## Time-to-effect summary

| Ingredient | Acute effect | Saturation effect |
|---|---|---|
| Caffeine | 20-45 min | n/a |
| Citrulline | 30-60 min | n/a |
| Beta-alanine | n/a (acute tingling is not the benefit) | 4-6 weeks |
| Creatine | n/a | 2-4 weeks |
| Sodium bicarbonate | 60-90 min | n/a |
| Glycerol | 30-60 min | n/a |

## A short answer to "should I take a pre-workout?"

- **If you're a beginner under-recovering / under-sleeping:** fix that first.
  Adding caffeine on top of bad sleep is a worse fix than fixing the sleep.
- **If you train fasted or in the early morning:** yes, a stim pre-workout
  reliably helps performance and is one of the most evidence-supported
  supplements for lifters.
- **If you train in the evening:** consider non-stim or nothing. Caffeine
  late ruins sleep, which ruins hypertrophy.
- **If you're already drinking 2-3 cups of coffee daily:** you're already
  caffeinated. Don't stack another 300 mg on top — diminishing returns and
  jitter.
