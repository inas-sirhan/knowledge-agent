# Supplement catalog — evidence-based picks for muscle building

A curated reference of the most relevant supplements for hypertrophy and lean
muscle gain, written for hardgainers / skinny lifters. Each entry includes the
recommended dose, typical monthly cost in USD, who it's best suited to, the
expected time-to-effect, and the strength of the underlying evidence.

Prices are rough US retail averages as of 2025 for generic / store-brand
versions. Branded "fancy" SKUs cost more without a meaningful quality bump for
the supplements rated **strong evidence**, where the molecule itself is the
active ingredient.

---

## Creatine monohydrate

- **Why it matters for muscle building:** the single most-studied performance
  supplement. Increases phosphocreatine in muscle, allowing slightly more reps
  per set and ~5–15% improvement in compound lift performance over weeks. Also
  draws water into muscle cells (small visual fullness boost).
- **Dose:** 3–5 g per day, every day. No need to "load" — same end-state in
  ~3–4 weeks at 5 g/day.
- **Form to buy:** plain creatine monohydrate powder. Avoid HCl, ethyl ester,
  "buffered" — they cost more for no proven benefit.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$5–10. Bulk powder is much cheaper than capsules.
- **Best for:** anyone training for hypertrophy or strength, especially
  beginners who don't yet have efficient ATP turnover. Vegetarians often see
  larger effects because dietary creatine is low.
- **Time to effect:** noticeable strength bump and ~1–2 kg scale-weight
  increase (water + glycogen) in 2–4 weeks. Hypertrophy benefit accrues over
  months.
- **Evidence level:** STRONG. Hundreds of RCTs.
- **Caveats:** drink enough water. Not for people with diagnosed kidney
  disease — talk to a doctor first.

## Whey protein (or plant blend if dairy-intolerant)

- **Why it matters:** convenient way to hit the ~1.6 g/kg/day protein target.
  Not magic — just food.
- **Dose:** 20–40 g per serving, 1–2 servings/day to fill gaps. Total daily
  protein matters more than timing.
- **Form to buy:** whey concentrate is fine for most. Whey isolate if
  lactose-sensitive. Pea+rice blend for vegans (combine for full amino profile).
- **Monthly cost:** ~$30–50 for ~2 kg (~30–60 servings).
- **Best for:** lifters under-eating protein from whole food. Skinny lifters
  often struggle to eat enough — shakes lower the friction.
- **Time to effect:** immediate, in the sense that hitting protein target is a
  prerequisite for hypertrophy. No "kick-in" period.
- **Evidence level:** STRONG (as a tool to hit protein, not as a magic muscle
  builder).
- **Caveats:** if you already hit 1.6 g/kg from food, additional whey is just
  expensive protein.

## Caffeine

- **Why it matters:** real, repeatable performance boost. Improves
  power output, reduces perceived effort, helps push past sticking points.
- **Dose:** 3–6 mg per kg bodyweight, 30–60 min pre-workout. For a 70 kg
  lifter that's ~200–400 mg.
- **Form to buy:** capsules / coffee / energy drink — all work. Pre-workout
  blends are mostly caffeine in a $40 jar.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$5 (capsules) to ~$60 (branded pre-workout).
- **Best for:** anyone who isn't already caffeine-sensitive or doesn't
  struggle with sleep.
- **Time to effect:** ~30 min, every workout.
- **Evidence level:** STRONG.
- **Caveats:** don't dose late in the day — half-life is ~5 hours; sleep
  damage will outweigh the workout gain. Tolerance develops; cycle off if
  effects fade.

## Beta-alanine

- **Why it matters:** raises muscle carnosine, buffering acidity. Helps with
  high-rep sets (~30–90 sec time-under-tension). Modest effect.
- **Dose:** 3–5 g/day, split into 2–3 doses to avoid the harmless tingling.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$10–15.
- **Best for:** intermediate lifters doing high-volume / drop sets. Limited
  benefit for low-rep strength work.
- **Time to effect:** 4–6 weeks to saturate muscle carnosine.
- **Evidence level:** MODERATE.
- **Caveats:** tingling (paresthesia) is harmless but uncomfortable; split
  doses or buy time-release.

## Citrulline malate

- **Why it matters:** modest improvement in rep volume on a given workout
  via nitric-oxide pathway. Mild "pump" feel.
- **Dose:** 6–8 g, 30–60 min pre-workout.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$20.
- **Best for:** intermediate-to-advanced lifters chasing the last 5% of
  workout volume. Lower priority than creatine/protein/caffeine.
- **Time to effect:** same-session pump; volume effect after a few sessions.
- **Evidence level:** MODERATE.

## Vitamin D3

- **Why it matters:** widespread deficiency in indoor populations, and
  deficiency suppresses testosterone and recovery. Correcting a deficiency can
  improve training response; supplementing beyond normal levels does not.
- **Dose:** 1000–2000 IU/day if your blood level is low. Get tested — don't
  guess.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$3–5.
- **Evidence level:** STRONG for correcting deficiency, NONE for "boosting"
  past normal range.

## Multivitamin

- **Why it matters:** insurance against minor dietary gaps. Not a performance
  supplement.
- **Monthly cost:** ~$5–10.
- **Evidence level:** WEAK as a performance/hypertrophy aid; reasonable for
  general nutrition if you eat poorly.

## Supplements that are mostly snake oil

These are widely sold but the evidence does not support a hypertrophy benefit
for healthy lifters who already eat enough protein:

- **BCAAs / EAAs as a standalone shake** — redundant if you hit your daily
  protein target from food + whey. The leucine you need is already there.
  Save the money.
- **Testosterone boosters / "natural anabolics"** (Tribulus, fenugreek,
  D-aspartic acid, etc.) — no consistent evidence of clinically meaningful
  testosterone or muscle increase in healthy men with normal levels.
- **Glutamine** — no hypertrophy benefit in healthy lifters.
- **HMB** — possibly useful for severe untrained or detrained populations;
  basically no benefit on top of adequate protein in trained lifters.
- **ZMA** — usually a magnesium + zinc + B6 combo; only useful if you're
  deficient, and you can get a multi for less.
- **"Mass gainers"** — typically just oats + whey + sugar at a 4× markup.
  Buy oats, milk, and whey separately.

## Budget tiers

- **Bare bones (~$15/month):** creatine + caffeine + vitamin D (if deficient).
  Covers the highest-leverage supplements; spend everything else on food.
- **Standard (~$40/month):** add whey protein for protein-target convenience.
- **Advanced (~$70/month):** add citrulline malate and/or beta-alanine if
  you're doing high-volume hypertrophy work and have already optimised diet,
  sleep, and program.

## A skinny lifter's priority list

1. **Eat enough.** Calculate maintenance + 250–500 kcal/day surplus. If the
   scale isn't moving up week-over-week, add 200 kcal.
2. **Protein target: ~1.6–2.2 g/kg bodyweight per day.** Whole food first,
   whey to fill gaps.
3. **Train hard and progressively overload.** Add reps or load to the same
   exercises each week.
4. **Sleep 7–9 hours.** Lost sleep cripples recovery and appetite.
5. **Add creatine + caffeine.** Cheap, well-evidenced, immediate help.
6. **Only then consider citrulline / beta-alanine.**

Anything earlier in that list outweighs anything later. A $200/month stack
won't outwork a half-eaten dinner.
