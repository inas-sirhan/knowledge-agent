# The science of pizza — what's happening on a molecular level

This document goes deeper than the "what to do" recipes. It's the
underlying chemistry and physics that explains why pizza techniques work.

## Gluten — the protein network

Wheat flour contains two proteins, **glutenin** and **gliadin**. They don't
do anything individually. But when you add water and mix:

- The proteins hydrate and unfold.
- Glutenin (long chains) and gliadin (small globules) link together.
- The result is **gluten**: an elastic, extensible network.

Gluten is what holds gas in dough. Without it, you have a flatbread — a
dense, cracker-like thing. With it, you have leavened bread.

### How gluten develops

Three mechanisms:

1. **Mechanical agitation** (kneading, mixing): physically forces proteins
   into contact, accelerating linkage. Speed of development depends on
   force.
2. **Time** (resting / autolysis): given enough time, proteins find each
   other and link even without mechanical help. Slow but real.
3. **Stretch-and-fold**: combines a small amount of mechanical work
   (folding) with time. Effective for high-hydration doughs where kneading
   is impractical.

### What ruins gluten

- **Too much salt up front**: salt tightens the network; if added before
  proteins have started linking, it can inhibit development. Some bakers
  add salt during the autolyse to prevent over-development. Most pizza
  recipes add salt at the start because flexibility is more important than
  pure development speed.
- **Acidity**: very low pH (sour dough, vinegar) weakens gluten over time.
- **Fats**: oil coats the proteins and inhibits linkage. This is why oil
  is added after the salt and water are mixed.
- **Tearing**: over-kneading can break gluten chains; the dough turns
  sticky again.

### Why high-protein flour matters

Protein percentage in flour is roughly: 9% (cake flour) → 11% (AP) → 12.5%
(bread) → 14% (high-gluten / King Arthur Sir Galahad).

The higher the protein, the more glutenin/gliadin available to form the
network. **For pizza**: bread flour or higher is the right floor. AP works
for soft, tender styles (grandma, Chicago tavern). Italian 00 has a similar
protein content to bread flour but a much finer grind that gives a more
delicate crumb texture.

## Fermentation — what yeast actually does

Yeast (Saccharomyces cerevisiae) is a single-celled fungus. It eats simple
sugars (glucose, fructose) and produces:

- **Carbon dioxide** (the rise).
- **Ethanol** (alcohol — boils off in the oven).
- **Aromatic compounds** (the bread smell).

### How fermentation generates flavour

A short ferment produces mostly CO2 and ethanol. **Long, cold
fermentation** is where the magic happens, because three things accumulate
over time:

1. **Maltose and glucose** from starch hydrolysis. Wheat flour contains
   amylase enzymes (and is often supplemented with diastatic malt powder)
   that break down starch into simple sugars during fermentation.
2. **Lactic and acetic acids** from lactobacilli bacteria that live in
   any flour. These bacteria multiply slowly at fridge temperatures and
   produce the slight tang of properly fermented bread.
3. **Aroma compounds**: esters, aldehydes, ketones formed by yeast
   metabolism. These are the deep, complex bread flavours.

The cold ferment is critical because at room temperature, yeast produces
CO2 fast (= big rise quickly) but doesn't generate as much of the
flavour compounds. In the fridge (3°C), yeast slows dramatically while
the lactobacilli keep producing acids. **Slow ferment = more flavour per
unit gas.**

This is why a 48-hour cold-fermented dough tastes dramatically better
than a 4-hour same-day dough. Same yeast, same flour — completely
different flavour profile.

## The Maillard reaction — browning, not burning

When dough enters a hot oven, several things happen simultaneously:

1. **Water evaporates** from the surface.
2. The surface temperature climbs from ~30°C (proofed dough) to **140°C
   and above**.
3. At 140°C / 285°F, the **Maillard reaction** begins.

Maillard browning is a complex reaction between amino acids (proteins)
and reducing sugars in the dough. It produces:

- The golden-brown colour.
- Hundreds of new aromatic compounds (the bread smell amplified).
- Caramelised, "savoury-sweet" flavours.

### What this means for pizza

- **You need enough oven heat to drive surface temperature past 140°C
  before the interior is done.** This is why baking pizza on a
  preheated steel matters: instant contact with hot metal raises the
  bottom surface temperature fast, triggering Maillard browning before
  the dough has time to dry out.
- **Sugar accelerates Maillard.** This is why NY-style dough with 2.5%
  sugar browns more readily at 600°F than Neapolitan dough (no sugar) at
  900°F. The 900°F oven brute-forces the browning; the 600°F oven needs
  chemical help.

## Caramelisation — different from Maillard

People conflate "browning" but there are two distinct reactions:

- **Maillard**: amino acid + reducing sugar → brown compounds. Starts
  ~140°C.
- **Caramelisation**: sugar alone, heat → brown compounds. Starts ~160°C.

Most pizza crust browning is Maillard. Pure caramelisation happens in
sugar-heavy applications (caramelised onions, the sugar on a Hawaiian
pineapple).

## Starch gelatinisation — why pizza needs to bake long enough

Starch in flour is in granules. When wet starch is heated above ~60°C:

1. The granules absorb water.
2. They swell and burst.
3. Released starch molecules form a gel.

This is **gelatinisation**, and it's what gives bread its set structure.
A pizza that comes out of the oven before the starch is fully gelatinised
has a gummy, doughy interior — even if the outside looks done.

The signal that gelatinisation is complete: the interior reaches ~95-100°C
(boiling water temp). A doughy-centred pizza is one where the bottom is
browned but the middle didn't reach this temperature.

This is why **a too-thick pizza on a too-hot oven is risky**: the bottom
chars before the middle finishes. The fix is either thinner dough, lower
heat, or longer bake.

## Why crust crisps — water migration

When the pizza enters a hot oven, water moves outward through the dough
toward the cooler surface. Water on the surface evaporates, leaving behind
a dry crust. The longer this dehydration continues, the crispier the
crust.

But — if the pizza sits in the oven too long, water from the inside
continues to evaporate, leaving the entire pie dry and crackery.

**The pizza window**: get the bottom Maillard-browned and surface-
dehydrated before the interior over-dries. This is what "perfectly cooked"
means — the right balance of crispness and tenderness.

## Why high heat works better than low and long

At high heat (Neapolitan oven at 900°F):

- Maillard browning starts almost immediately on the surface.
- The interior heats fast but doesn't have time to dehydrate.
- Total bake time: 60-90 seconds.
- Result: crispy outside, tender inside.

At low heat (350°F):

- Maillard browning is slow.
- The interior dries out before the outside browns properly.
- Total bake time: 15+ minutes.
- Result: tough, cracker-dry, no Maillard depth.

Pizza is fundamentally a **fast, high-heat food**.

## Why fat matters in the dough

Even a small amount of oil (2-3% of flour weight) in NY-style dough does
three things:

1. **Tenderises** by interfering with gluten development (creating shorter,
   weaker chains).
2. **Aids browning** by transferring heat efficiently to the dough surface
   (oil at the contact point with a steel transfers heat better than
   dry dough).
3. **Adds flavour** if the oil is good (use real olive oil).

Neapolitan has no oil because the 900°F heat handles everything; NY needs
the oil to compensate for lower temperatures.

## Why salt is critical

- **Flavour**: 2.5% salt is roughly seawater concentration. It's the
  threshold below which bread tastes flat to most palates.
- **Yeast control**: salt slows yeast activity by ~30%, which is essential
  for long ferments. Without salt, dough over-proofs in hours.
- **Gluten strengthening**: salt tightens the protein network slightly,
  improving structure.

## A unified mental model

Pizza is a system. Each variable affects the others:

- **More water** → harder to handle, more open crumb, longer to bake.
- **More flour protein** → more structure, more elastic, longer ferment
  tolerance.
- **More yeast** → faster rise, less flavour.
- **More time (cold ferment)** → more flavour, more digestibility, harder
  to time.
- **More oven heat** → faster bake, more Maillard, smaller window of
  perfection.
- **More fat** → tender, browning-aided, longer shelf life.

The styles are not arbitrary. They are local equilibria within this
multi-variable system, each optimised for the equipment and tastes of a
specific time and place.

Understanding the science means you can perturb any single variable and
predict what happens, instead of just following recipes. That's the
difference between cooking pizza and engineering it.
