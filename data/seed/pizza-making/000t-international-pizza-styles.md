# International pizza styles — what pizza looks like outside Italy and the US

Pizza is a global food now. Every country that has it has adapted it. Some
adaptations are beautiful; some are surprising. Here are the meaningful
non-American, non-Italian styles worth knowing about.

## Argentine pizza — fugazza, fugazzeta, and the cheese mountain

Argentina has its own deep pizza culture, rooted in late-19th-century
Italian (especially Neapolitan and Sicilian) immigration to Buenos Aires.
Argentinian pizza is its own thing.

### Fugazza

A focaccia-style thick pizza base, topped with mozzarella and a **huge
amount of onion** (sliced into thick strips, oil-softened, almost
caramelised). Often no tomato sauce at all.

### Fugazzeta

Fugazza taken further: **two layers of dough** with cheese stuffed
between, then topped with mozzarella and more onion on top. The cheese
melts into the dough seam. Heavier than a traditional pizza but
fantastically rich.

### Pizza por porción / pizza al molde

Sold by the slice ("por porción") or by the whole pan ("al molde"). Thick,
heavily-cheesed, often with two layers of cheese (a base + a topping).
The aesthetic is **more cheese than you think reasonable**.

### Argentine sauce style

Tomato-light. Often almost just a touch of tomato pulp under the cheese.
The cheese is the star.

### Where to try it

If you're ever in Buenos Aires: **Pizzería Güerrín** (since 1932) for
the legacy version, **El Cuartito** (since 1934) for the slice-shop
experience.

## Brazilian pizza

Brazil takes pizza in a different direction. Pizzas in São Paulo and Rio
are often **enormous** (40+ cm), thin-ish, lavishly topped with unusual
combinations including:

- **Catupiry cheese** (a Brazilian processed cream cheese, gooey and mild)
  on basically everything.
- **Banana + cinnamon + sugar + cheese** as a dessert pizza (the
  "pizza doce") — yes, with cheese, and it works.
- **Heart of palm** (palmito) with chicken and Catupiry — one of the most
  popular savoury pies.
- **Calabresa**: a Brazilian cured sausage, the equivalent of pepperoni
  but with different spicing.

São Paulo is genuinely a great pizza city — different from Italian or
American but worth taking seriously. Brazilian pizzerias often serve
**pizza rodízio**: an all-you-can-eat format where servers bring slices
of different varieties to your table continuously.

## Japanese pizza

Sushi pizza isn't really a thing in Japan; it's a North American invention
that gets attributed to Japan. But **Japanese-style pizza** does exist as
an adaptation:

- **Mochi pizza**: dough made with mochi rice flour for a chewy texture.
- **Mayo on pizza**: a Japanese stylistic choice — drizzles of Japanese
  mayo (Kewpie, sweeter than American mayo) on top of pizza. Polarising.
- **Corn + tuna**: a classic Japanese pizza topping. Sounds odd; works.
- **Okonomi-style pizza**: pizza-okonomiyaki fusion with cabbage, bonito
  flakes, and Japanese mayo.

Don't dismiss Japanese pizza. The cultural confidence of "we'll put what we
want on this" is the same confidence that gave the US the pepperoni pizza.

## Korean pizza

The Korean pizza chain scene (Pizza Hut Korea, Mr Pizza, Pizza Maru) is
its own universe:

- **Sweet potato mousse** on the crust edge.
- **Bulgogi pizza**: marinated beef as a topping. Genuinely excellent.
- **Stuffed crusts** with cheese, sweet potato, even sweet bean paste.
- **Honey butter drizzle** topping.

Korean pizza is **dramatic** — visually loaded, flavor-loaded, sometimes
with shrimp tails poking out of the crust edge. American pizzerias quietly
borrowed the cheese-stuffed crust concept from Korean chains.

## German pizza ("Flammkuchen" / tarte flambée)

The Alsatian/German equivalent: a very thin, very crispy unleavened flatbread
topped with **crème fraîche, onions, and bacon (lardons)**. Not technically
pizza but always discussed alongside it. Wood-fired oven, 90-second cook,
served immediately.

It's a great template for home cooks: low-effort dough (no yeast required
in some recipes), bold smoky-creamy topping, fast bake.

## Turkish pizza — lahmacun and pide

- **Lahmacun**: a very thin flatbread topped with minced lamb, parsley,
  onion, peppers, served with lemon and rolled up and eaten. Pizza-adjacent
  but its own dish.
- **Pide**: a boat-shaped flatbread closed at the ends, often with cheese
  and ground meat. Eats like an open-faced pizza.

Both are pre-Italian, ancient flatbread traditions in their own right —
sometimes referred to as "Turkish pizza" by Western menus, but the locals
don't really call them pizza.

## French pizza — beyond tarte flambée

French pizzerias (especially in the south) make a Provençal-style pizza
with:

- **Niçoise olives** and **anchovy fillets**.
- **Pissaladière**: Niçoise specialty — caramelised onions, anchovies,
  olives, on a thin dough. A close cousin to pizza, technically a
  flatbread.
- **Fougasse**: not pizza, but a related Provençal yeasted flatbread,
  sometimes topped pizza-style with olives and herbs.

## Lebanese / Middle Eastern flatbreads — manakish

Manakish is the breakfast flatbread of Lebanon, Syria, Palestine, Jordan.
Topped with:

- **Za'atar + olive oil** — the most common version. A herb-and-seed
  mixture (thyme, sumac, sesame) mixed with olive oil and baked on dough.
- **Cheese** (akkawi, halloumi).
- **Spinach** with onion and sumac (sbanekh).
- **Lahm bi ajeen**: minced meat with spices.

Manakish predates pizza by centuries and represents the oldest living
"flatbread + topping baked" tradition. The technique and equipment are
remarkably similar to modern pizza — pre-Italian flatbreads have a long
history.

## Indian pizza

Indian-style pizza incorporates:

- **Paneer** (Indian cheese, doesn't melt — used as a topping cube).
- **Tikka chicken**, **butter chicken**, or other curry chicken styles.
- **Coriander, mint, garlic** as topping flavours.
- **Naan or kulcha** sometimes used as the dough base.

Domino's India has a different menu than Domino's US, with these kinds of
toppings.

## Australian pizza

Australia has its own pizza vocabulary. Toppings include:

- **Egg** (much earlier and more accepted on pizza than in the US).
- **BBQ chicken**, often with red onion and cilantro.
- **Aussie pizza**: bacon, egg, and ham.
- **Hawaiian** is normalised here without the cultural argument it gets
  in the US.

The Australian pizza scene leans American with some Italian-adjacent
flavour profiles.

## Israeli / Middle Eastern fusion

Tel Aviv has a young, experimental pizza scene:

- **Za'atar dough** (mixed with za'atar herbs before baking).
- **Labneh** as a sauce alternative.
- **Pomegranate molasses + lamb** as a finishing drizzle on lamb pizzas.

## Where the global pizza scene is headed

A few patterns across the international scene:

1. **Localising the toppings to whatever's culturally familiar.** Korean
   pizzerias put bulgogi on pizza; Indian pizzerias put paneer tikka.
   This isn't worse — it's just regional confidence.

2. **Mixing pizza with other dough traditions.** Mochi pizza in Japan,
   za'atar pizza in Israel, naan pizza in India — pizza is becoming a
   general-purpose flatbread format that can host any tradition.

3. **The cheese question is universal.** Every culture has had to decide
   how much and what kind of cheese to put on pizza. Argentina says "more."
   Japan says "with mayo." Italy says "a specific small amount." Each
   answer is legitimate.

## What this means for home cooks

The reason to know about international styles isn't to copy them
verbatim. It's to expand the imagination of what "pizza" can be. Some
combinations that look weird on paper (banana + cinnamon + cheese?) are
actually delicious. The cultural confidence to try unconventional
combinations is half the joy of cooking pizza at home.

If you're tired of margherita and pepperoni, look at how the rest of the
world does it. Try a fugazzeta this weekend. Try manakish with za'atar.
Try a Korean-style stuffed crust. Each one teaches you something the
classics can't.
