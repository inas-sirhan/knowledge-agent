# Yeast and pre-ferments — the engine of pizza flavour

If dough fundamentals are the engine specification, this document is about
the fuel: how much yeast, in what form, and whether to give it a head start.

## Yeast types compared

| Type | Active component | Strength vs IDY | When to use |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Instant dry yeast (IDY)** | Dry granules with surfactant coat | Reference (1×) | Modern standard. Add directly to dough. |
| **Active dry yeast (ADY)** | Same yeast, different coating | ~80% (use 1.25×) | Older recipes. Needs proofing in warm water first. |
| **Fresh / cake yeast** | Live yeast paste, refrigerated | ~33% (use 3×) | Traditional Neapolitan. Best flavour per unit weight, hardest to find. |
| **Sourdough starter** | Wild yeast + lactobacilli culture | Variable | When you want sour tang + complex flavour. |

For most home pizza, instant dry yeast is the right default. SAF Red and
Fleischmann's Instant are widely available and reliable. Buy in a 500 g
brick, refrigerate after opening — it lasts a year.

## How much yeast

The single most important yeast decision is **how much**:

| Yeast % (instant) | Ferment time at room temp | Use case |
|---|---|---|
| 1.0% | 1–2 hours | Same-day "emergency" dough |
| 0.5% | 2–4 hours | Quick weeknight pizza |
| 0.3% | 4–8 hours | Same-day Neapolitan |
| 0.1–0.2% | 18–48 hour cold ferment | Pizzeria-quality |
| 0.05% | 48–72 hour cold ferment | Long-ferment, deep-flavour |

Lower yeast + longer ferment = more flavour. The difference between a
2-hour rise and a 48-hour rise on the same flour is night and day.

## Pre-ferments — what they do and why

A **pre-ferment** is a small portion of the recipe's flour, water, and
yeast that's mixed 12-24 hours before the final dough and allowed to
ferment on its own. The next day, the pre-ferment is mixed into the final
dough.

What this buys you:

1. **Better flavour** — the pre-ferment is essentially a 12-24 hour ferment
   on a portion of the dough, developing deep flavour compounds that the
   short final ferment can't match.
2. **Better extensibility** — the long pre-ferment relaxes a portion of the
   gluten, making the final dough easier to stretch.
3. **Better crumb structure** — open, irregular alveoli (the holes in the
   cornicione).
4. **Better browning and crust character** — the acids developed in the
   pre-ferment caramelise more readily in the oven.

You can do all of this with a long cold ferment of the full dough, and many
home cooks do. But pre-ferments give you a similar quality boost on a
**same-day** dough.

### Poolish

The French/Polish pre-ferment. **Equal weights of flour and water**, plus a
tiny amount of yeast. Loose, batter-like consistency.

```
100 g  bread flour
100 g  water
  0.3 g  instant yeast (a pinch)
```

Mix the night before. Cover, leave at room temperature. After 12-16 hours
it'll be bubbly, domed, smelling lightly fermented, with a hint of sweetness.

Use this in your final dough by adding the entire poolish to the mix and
subtracting the poolish flour and water from the total recipe quantities.

Poolish is a great match for **NY-style** pizza: it adds depth without
making the dough sour, and the high hydration of the poolish itself
contributes to a softer interior.

### Biga

The Italian pre-ferment. **Lower hydration** than poolish — typically
50-55% water-to-flour. Drier, doughier consistency.

```
100 g  bread flour or 00 flour
 50 g  water
  0.3 g  instant yeast (a pinch)
```

Mix the night before (or even 18-24 hours ahead). It'll feel like
sweetened dough, with a yeasty-fruity aroma.

Biga is traditional for **Roman al taglio** and many **Neapolitan**
recipes. It gives the dough a stronger, chewier structure with a slightly
more pronounced sour-fruity flavour.

### Sourdough starter (levain)

A culture of wild yeast and lactobacilli, fed regularly with flour and
water. Replaces commercial yeast entirely. Adds:

- **Tang** (lactic and acetic acid from the bacteria).
- **Longer keeping** (the acidity preserves the bread).
- **Deeper flavour** (much more complex than commercial yeast).
- **Slower rise** (the wild yeast is less aggressive than commercial).

For pizza, use roughly **15-20% starter** of your total flour weight, and
expect to **double or triple your ferment time** compared to a
commercial-yeast equivalent.

```
1000 g  bread flour
 650 g  water (65%)
  20 g  salt
 150 g  starter at peak (15%)
```

Bulk ferment 4-6 hours at room temperature. Cold ferment 24-48 hours.
The flavour is dramatically different from commercial-yeast pizza —
sourer, more bread-like.

If you don't already keep a sourdough starter, this isn't the project to
start one for. A commercial yeast pizza with a 48-hour cold ferment will
get you ~90% of the flavour benefit at 5% of the work.

## Same-day vs cold ferment — when to use which

**Same-day** (room-temperature ferment, 2-6 hours):

- When you have less than 24 hours of lead time.
- Use higher yeast (0.3-0.5%) to compensate.
- Consider adding a pre-ferment (poolish/biga) for a quality boost.
- Acceptable for grandma, Detroit, Sicilian. Less ideal for NY.

**Cold ferment** (24-72 hours):

- The single biggest quality upgrade you can do.
- Use very low yeast (0.1-0.2%).
- Bulk ferment 1 hour at room temperature first to start activity, **then**
  refrigerate.
- Bring back to room temp 1.5-2 hours before shaping (cold dough won't
  stretch).
- The improvement is real and noticeable.

**Long cold ferment** (72-96 hours):

- Diminishing returns past 72 hours. Dough may start to over-ferment and
  taste alcoholic.
- For experimentation, not for a regular weeknight rhythm.

## Reading dough readiness

Yeast doesn't follow the clock; it follows temperature and food. The same
dough at 24°C ferments faster than at 18°C. Recipes are guidelines, not
laws.

Read the dough itself:

- **Properly fermented**: domed, light to lift, springs back slowly when
  poked, smells yeasty and sweet.
- **Under-fermented**: tight, dense, springs back immediately, doughy
  smell with no yeast aroma.
- **Over-fermented**: sticky, sagging, sour or alcoholic smell, may have
  surface bubbles popping. Salvage by knocking down and reshaping; result
  will be flatter, sourer.

## Cold ferment storage

For 24-72 hour cold ferment:

- Use a sealed container or covered tray. Dough exposed to fridge air
  will skin over.
- Lightly oil the dough surface to prevent sticking to the lid.
- Pre-portion into balls before refrigerating, OR refrigerate the bulk and
  divide on the day of baking. The former gives more uniform results.

## A simple decision framework

If you've never made pizza dough before:

1. **First attempt**: 0.5% yeast, 4-hour room temperature ferment. Goal is
   to learn the process.
2. **Second attempt**: 0.2% yeast, 24-hour cold ferment. Now you'll taste
   the difference cold fermentation makes.
3. **Third attempt**: 0.2% yeast, 48-hour cold ferment with a small biga.
   This is where home pizza starts approaching pizzeria quality.

Don't jump to step 3 first. The point of pizza-making is iteration — each
batch teaches you the next.
