# Detroit-style pizza

Detroit pizza is the most underrated American style and the easiest deeply
satisfying pizza to make in a normal home oven. It's a rectangular,
deep-pan pie with a thick airy interior and — most importantly — a **frico
crust**: a band of cheese that has melted down between the dough and the
pan walls, then caramelised into a crispy lacy edge. That edge is the
prize.

## Origin and identity

Detroit-style pizza was born at Buddy's Rendezvous on Conant and Six Mile
Road in Detroit in 1946. The traditional pan was an industrial-supply
**blue steel automotive drip tray** — a deep, rectangular tin used in Detroit's
auto factories. Those pans developed character over decades of seasoning
that home cooks can never quite replicate, which is why people now buy
purpose-made Detroit pans from Lloyd Pans (the de facto standard) or from
Detroit Style Pizza Co.

The style is defined by:

- A **rectangular pan**, traditionally 10×14 inches, with sides ~5 cm tall.
- **Cheese all the way to the edge** of the pan, so it melts down and
  caramelises against the pan walls (the frico).
- **Brick cheese** — a Wisconsin cheese with a high fat content and good
  browning, traditionally specific to Detroit-style. Aged brick has more
  funk; young brick is closer to mozzarella. A 50/50 brick-and-low-moisture-
  mozz blend is a common compromise outside the Midwest.
- **Sauce on top, after the bake** — "stripes" or dollops of sauce. This
  inversion (sauce on top instead of under cheese) is the defining American
  trait of the style, alongside Chicago deep dish.
- **A thick, airy, focaccia-like interior** that's tall but light, with
  large irregular crumb holes.

## Dough

Detroit dough is higher hydration than NY (~70–75%) and proofs in the pan
itself. The high hydration is what gives the interior that big, open,
airy crumb.

```
500 g  bread flour
350 g  water (70% hydration)
12  g  salt (2.4%)
 3  g  instant yeast (0.6%)
 25 g  olive oil
```

1. Combine water, salt, yeast, oil. Add flour. Mix to shaggy dough.
2. Rest 20 min, knead 5 min (it'll be wet; use wet hands or a stand mixer).
3. Bulk ferment 60 min at room temperature.
4. Coat a Detroit pan generously with olive oil (3–4 tbsp). Plop the dough
   into the pan. With oiled hands, gently press it toward the edges — it
   won't reach the corners, that's fine.
5. Cover and proof at room temperature 60–90 min, gently re-pressing toward
   corners every 30 min. After 90 min the dough should fill the pan.
6. **Optional but recommended**: a 24-hour cold proof before step 4 for
   better flavour. After step 3, refrigerate the bulk dough 24 hours, then
   pan-proof for 2 hours.

## Assembly — the order matters

This is where Detroit differs from everything else:

1. Dough proofed in oiled pan ✓.
2. **Pepperoni first** (optional) — directly on the dough. Use small-cup
   pepperoni so it cups and chars.
3. **Cheese second** — cubed or hand-torn brick (or mozz/brick blend) in a
   thick layer that goes **all the way to the pan edges**. The cheese against
   the metal is what creates the frico crust.
4. **Bake** — 500°F / 260°C, lower rack, ~13–17 minutes. Watch the corners;
   they should be deeply caramelised, not burnt.
5. **Sauce after** — once out of the oven, stripe or dollop sauce over the
   top. Don't cover it — you want stripes of red, not a sauce layer.
6. **Finish**: grated parmesan, dried oregano, a drizzle of olive oil.

## Sauce

Detroit sauce is a thick, slightly sweet, simply-seasoned tomato sauce —
similar to NY but often a touch sweeter and chunkier.

```
1 can (28 oz) crushed tomatoes
3 tbsp tomato paste
1 tbsp sugar
1 tbsp olive oil
1 clove garlic, minced
1 tsp dried oregano
1 tsp salt
```

Simmer 20 min uncovered to reduce. Cool. Apply *after* baking.

## Cheese — the brick question

Authentic brick cheese (Widmer's is the canonical brand) has a high fat
content and unique melting behaviour that's central to the style. Outside
the Midwest it's hard to find. Workable substitutes:

- **50% low-moisture mozzarella + 50% mild cheddar or Monterey Jack** —
  closest behaviour, browns well.
- **Mozzarella + Muenster** — closer to brick's flavour, still browns.
- **Mozzarella only** — works but lacks the funky depth.

Whatever cheese you use, **don't shred it pre-bought**. Hand-cube or hand-tear
fresh. Pre-shredded won't melt the same way.

## The "edge cheese" technique

The frico crust is the soul of the style. To maximise it:

1. Use a high-fat cheese (the fat fries against the pan).
2. Press cheese against the pan walls deliberately when you assemble.
3. Make sure the pan is generously oiled — the cheese fries in this oil.
4. Bake at high heat (500°F) and low rack (so the bottom and edges crisp
   before the centre over-bakes).
5. Let the pizza rest 5 minutes after coming out of the oven — the frico
   sets and detaches from the pan more easily.

## Pan and equipment

- **The pan matters.** Lloyd Pans 10×14 (or 8×10 for a smaller pie) is the
  standard. The hard-anodized aluminium is non-stick enough that the
  pizza releases cleanly. Generic steel pans work but you have to oil them
  more aggressively.
- **A flexible offset spatula** to slide between pizza and pan edge.
- **A wire rack** to cool the pizza on so the bottom doesn't steam.

## Common Detroit mistakes

- **Cheese not to the pan edge.** No edge cheese = no frico = a sad
  rectangular pizza. Push the cheese against the walls.
- **Sauce baked in.** Sauce goes on **after** the bake. If you bake it on
  top, you've made Sicilian, not Detroit.
- **Under-proofed dough.** If the dough doesn't fill the pan and rise
  noticeably during the 60–90 min proof, you'll get a dense, bready
  interior. Give it time.
- **Over-baked.** Once the corners are deep brown, get it out. The interior
  finishes in carry-over heat. Detroit can go from perfect to bitter-burnt in
  90 seconds.
- **Cold dough into hot pan.** Always proof in the pan at room temperature.

## Why Detroit is the best beginner pizza

- The pan does most of the work. No round-stretching anxiety.
- The high hydration is forgiving — even sloppy dough comes out airy.
- The 500°F oven is what every home oven hits.
- It looks impressive (rectangular, golden, caramel edges) and tastes great
  even with imperfect technique.
- The components (brick cheese aside) are pantry-friendly.

If you've never made pizza, start with Detroit. You will produce something
visibly excellent on the first or second attempt.
