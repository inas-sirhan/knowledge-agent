# Diagnostics and troubleshooting — fix what went wrong

This is the field guide. Match your problem to one of the headings below and
follow the most likely causes. The order of causes within each section is
"check this first."

## The bottom is soggy / pale

Most common pizza problem at home.

Likely causes:

1. **Under-preheated stone/steel.** Solution: preheat 60 minutes minimum.
2. **No stone/steel at all** — baking on a tray. Solution: get a steel.
3. **Sauce too wet.** Solution: cook the sauce down to "spoonable, not
   pourable" before applying.
4. **Over-topped, especially with wet toppings** (fresh mozz, fresh
   tomato, raw mushrooms). Solution: pre-cook or dry these.
5. **Bottom rack used instead of upper.** The bottom of most home ovens is
   the heating element zone — moves heat upward, not down. Solution: bake
   on an upper rack.
6. **Pizza taken out too early.** Solution: bake until you can lift the
   edge with a peel and see browning underneath.

## The bottom is burnt / black

Likely causes:

1. **Stone/steel placed directly on a too-hot bottom element.** Move higher.
2. **Bake time too long for your oven.** Solution: shorten by 1-2 min, see
   if the top is still cooked.
3. **Sugar in the dough.** Sugar caramelises and browns; with high heat it
   burns. Solution: reduce sugar to 1% or eliminate (if you're making
   true Neapolitan).
4. **Cornmeal on the peel.** Cornmeal that falls onto the steel between
   pizzas burns and adds bitterness. Use semolina (higher burn point) or
   parchment for first bakes.

## The cheese isn't browning

Likely causes:

1. **Oven not hot enough.** Solution: preheat longer; use broil for last
   90 seconds.
2. **Cheese is pre-shredded.** The anti-caking agent prevents browning.
   Solution: buy block, shred yourself.
3. **Too thick a cheese layer.** Heat can't penetrate. Solution: less
   cheese, distributed more evenly.
4. **Rack too low.** Cheese browns from radiant top-down heat. Solution:
   upper rack.

## The cheese is burnt before the crust is done

Likely causes:

1. **Rack too high.** Top is over-exposed. Solution: middle rack, longer
   bake.
2. **Cheese on too long.** Solution: in pan styles, add cheese after the
   first few minutes of bake.
3. **Wrong cheese for the temperature.** Some low-moisture mozz browns
   very fast. Solution: blend with provolone or a milder mozz.

## The dough won't stretch / springs back

Likely causes:

1. **Dough is cold.** Solution: 1.5-2 hours at room temperature before
   shaping.
2. **Under-fermented.** Solution: longer ferment next time.
3. **Over-kneaded.** Solution: less kneading.
4. **Dough rested in a tight ball.** Solution: pre-shape and rest 20 min
   before final stretch.

## The dough tears when stretched

Likely causes:

1. **Over-fermented dough.** Gluten breakdown. Solution: shorter ferment.
2. **Too thin in the middle.** Solution: leave a slightly thicker centre
   when stretching.
3. **Hydration too high for the flour.** Solution: switch to bread flour
   or reduce water by 2-3%.

## The cornicione doesn't puff

Likely causes:

1. **Oven not hot enough.** Solution: hotter oven (need 800°F+ for true
   Neapolitan puff). Without an outdoor oven, you won't get true Neapolitan
   puff.
2. **Stone/steel too cool.** Solution: longer preheat.
3. **Dough degassed by rolling pin.** Solution: stretch by hand, not a pin.
4. **Cheese to the edge.** Cheese weight prevents puff. Solution: leave a
   1 cm border.

## The pizza sticks to the peel during launching

Likely causes:

1. **Too long on the peel before launching.** Dough absorbs moisture from
   the peel. Solution: assemble fast (under 60 seconds) and launch
   immediately.
2. **Sticky high-hydration dough on a dry peel.** Solution: dust the peel
   with semolina (better than flour — won't burn).
3. **Wet sauce or wet cheese leaking through the dough.** Solution: don't
   leave the pizza on the peel for long, or use parchment under the pizza
   for the launch.

A bulletproof method: **assemble the pizza on parchment paper** placed on
the peel. Launch with the parchment underneath. After 2 minutes the dough
sets — slide the parchment out at that point.

## The pizza is uneven (one side burnt, one side raw)

Likely causes:

1. **Hot spots in the oven.** All home ovens have them. Solution: rotate
   the pizza 180° at the midway point.
2. **Cold spots.** Solution: rotate, and consider moving the stone closer
   to the back wall.

## The crust is tough / dense

Likely causes:

1. **Under-fermented.** Solution: longer ferment.
2. **Over-kneaded.** Solution: less kneading.
3. **Wrong flour.** Bread flour where AP would be better, or vice versa.
4. **Hydration too low.** Solution: 2-3% more water.
5. **Too much salt.** Solution: 2.5% max.

## The flavour is flat / bland

Likely causes:

1. **Short ferment.** Solution: cold ferment 24-72 hours.
2. **Too much yeast.** Solution: less yeast (0.2% or below), longer time.
3. **No salt or insufficient salt.** Solution: 2.5-3% salt. Confirm by
   tasting raw dough — should taste seasoned, not bland.
4. **Sauce under-seasoned.** Solution: salt + sugar + oregano + garlic +
   olive oil. Taste before applying.

## The pizza is dense at the bottom but airy at the top

This is normal but excessive density suggests:

1. **Pizza launched onto a stone that wasn't hot enough.** Solution: longer
   preheat. The bottom should set in the first 30 seconds.
2. **Too long on the peel before launching.** Solution: faster assembly.

## The dough proofs but doesn't fill the pan

For pan styles (Detroit, Sicilian, grandma):

1. **Not enough proof time.** Solution: 60-90 min at room temp, redistributing
   to corners every 30 min.
2. **Pan too oily, dough slides.** Some oil is necessary; too much and the
   dough can't grip the walls. Solution: 2-3 tbsp olive oil in a
   standard Detroit pan.
3. **Dough cold.** Pull out of the fridge 2 hours before panning.

## The pizza tastes off / sour

Likely causes:

1. **Over-fermented.** Smell will tell you — alcoholic, sour, no fresh
   yeast aroma. Solution: shorter ferment next time.
2. **Sourdough was over-active.** Use less starter, or use it sooner.
3. **Bad oil.** Use fresh, quality olive oil.

## The toppings slide off during the cut

Likely causes:

1. **Too much sauce.** Cheese floats on the sauce, slides on slicing.
2. **Pre-shredded cheese (won't melt-bind).** Solution: shred your own.
3. **Cut too soon.** Solution: rest 2-5 minutes after baking; the cheese
   re-sets.

## The crust is too thick / too thin

Mostly: dough portion size and stretching technique.

- **Too thick**: smaller dough ball, stretch wider.
- **Too thin (tears)**: bigger ball, or higher-protein flour.

## A diagnostic question framework

When something goes wrong, ask in order:

1. **Was the oven hot enough? (Stone/steel saturated?)**
2. **Was the dough properly fermented?**
3. **Was the sauce reduced?**
4. **Was the cheese fresh-shredded?**
5. **Was the pie over-topped?**
6. **Was the rack height correct?**

90% of home pizza problems answer "no" to one of those six. Identify the
"no", fix that variable, and re-test.

## When to give up on a dough

Sometimes you've under-fermented and the dough is dense and unworkable.
Or over-fermented and it's sticky and sour. Don't ruin pizza night
salvaging it.

- If under-fermented: just let it rest 1-2 more hours and try again, or
  cold-ferment it 24 hours and bake tomorrow.
- If over-fermented: salvage by adding a fresh batch of dough (knock it
  down, fold in a new batch of fresh dough at 25% ratio, re-ferment 1-2
  hours). Or just bake it — it'll be flat, sour, but edible.

## When to give up on a pizza

If the bottom is burnt and the top is raw — you're done. Don't try to
flip it to finish the top; you'll just burn the cheese too. Accept the
loss, eat what's edible (top), order pizza next time.

Pizza is iterative. Every failed pie teaches you something. Take notes
on what changed each time and you'll converge on your perfect home pie
within 5-10 attempts.
