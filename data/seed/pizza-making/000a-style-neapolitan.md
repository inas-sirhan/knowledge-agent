# Neapolitan pizza — the canonical style

Neapolitan pizza is the original. Every other style in this knowledge base is
either a descendant of it (NY, bar pie), an American adaptation (Detroit,
Sicilian-American), or a deliberate reinvention (California). Understanding
how Neapolitan works gives you a baseline for understanding everything else.

## What it actually is

A Neapolitan pie is small (~30 cm diameter), thin in the middle, with a
puffed, char-blistered, soft outer ring (the **cornicione**). The centre is
deliberately wet and droopy — you eat it folded into quarters by hand
("a libretto") or with a knife and fork. The cooking time at the canonical
heat is 60–90 seconds. Done. The crust should have **leopard spots** — small,
distinct black char marks on the cornicione — not a solid burnt black, not a
uniform brown.

If you've only had American pizza, the first Neapolitan pizza you eat can
feel disorienting: too wet in the middle, too charred, too small, not enough
cheese. That's the style working correctly. It's a different idea than NY.

## Dough — the rules

True Neapolitan dough is austere:

- **Type 00 flour** (a finely ground soft Italian wheat flour with a protein
  content around 11–13%). Caputo Pizzeria, Caputo Saccorosso, and Polselli
  Classica are the standards. The fine grind gives a tender crumb and the
  protein level supports the long ferment without going slack.
- **Water**, ~58–65% hydration (baker's percentage — i.e. water weight is
  58–65% of flour weight).
- **Sea salt**, ~2.5–3% of flour weight.
- **Fresh yeast** (not instant). About 0.1–0.3% by weight for a long ferment.
- **No oil, no sugar, no milk, no eggs.** The dough is four ingredients.

Mix the flour, water, and salt; rest 20 minutes (autolyse); add the yeast;
knead by hand or stand mixer for about 10–15 minutes until smooth and
slightly elastic; bulk ferment at room temperature for 2 hours; divide into
~230–280 g balls; let the balls ferment another 4–8 hours at room temperature
(in a covered, lightly floured tray with space between them) **or** 24–72
hours in the fridge for a cold ferment with more flavour development.

## The oven and the cook

The defining technical fact of Neapolitan pizza is the heat. AVPN-spec ovens
hit **430–485°C / 805–905°F** on the floor and roughly equivalent in the
dome. At those temperatures, the bottom of the pizza sets and chars before
the inside has time to dry out. **That's why the middle stays wet** — it's a
feature of the heat profile, not a bug.

This is also why Neapolitan is the hardest style to replicate in a standard
home oven. A standard residential oven maxes out at 550°F / 290°C, and at
that temperature you cannot finish the bottom in 90 seconds. You either:

1. Get an outdoor pizza oven (Ooni, Gozney, Roccbox — all hit 900°F) — by
   far the easiest solution.
2. Use a baking steel preheated for an hour, then broil on the top rack —
   you'll get close but not quite. Bake time will be 3–5 minutes.
3. Accept that "home Neapolitan" in a standard oven is a different animal
   than the original.

## Cheese, sauce, toppings

- **Sauce**: crushed San Marzano DOP tomatoes (or any quality whole peeled
  tomatoes from southern Italy), with a pinch of salt. No cooking, no
  seasoning. The 90 seconds in the oven is the cook. If you cook the sauce
  beforehand, by the time it's been in a 900°F oven it tastes burnt-jam.
- **Cheese**: fior di latte (cow's-milk mozzarella) is the standard. Buffalo
  mozzarella is more traditional and richer but releases more water; use it
  if you want, but drain it well or you'll wet the pie. Cube the cheese (1
  cm cubes) and distribute lightly — not edge to edge.
- **Basil**: a few fresh leaves, added after the bake (high heat destroys
  fresh basil's flavour).
- **Olive oil**: a thin drizzle of extra virgin after the bake.
- **Toppings list per pizza is short**: Margherita (basil, mozz, tomato),
  Marinara (no cheese — tomato, garlic, oregano, oil), or one or two other
  ingredients. The style does not bear heavy load — the wet centre needs
  light toppings or the structure collapses.

## How to know yours came out right

- Cornicione is puffed, ~2 cm tall, with leopard spots (not solid black).
- The bottom is dry but flexible, with a few char spots.
- The middle is wet enough that the pizza droops when you lift a slice
  ("flop"). Fold it lengthwise to eat.
- The cheese is melted but not browned.
- The flavour is bright tomato, milky cheese, slight char.

## Common Neapolitan mistakes

- **Sauce too watered down.** Use thick crushed tomatoes, not passata.
- **Too much cheese.** A Margherita gets ~80–100 g cheese, not 200 g.
- **Cheese touching the edge.** Cheese should sit in the middle so the
  cornicione has room to puff. Cheese to the edge weighs the rim down.
- **Cooking too long.** If your home oven needs 5 minutes to finish a
  Neapolitan-style pie, the middle will dry out. Accept the difference;
  consider an outdoor oven if Neapolitan is your goal.
- **Stretching the dough thinner than 3 mm.** Too thin and it tears under
  the wet centre.

## A 24-hour Neapolitan dough recipe (4 pizzas, ~250 g each)

```
600  g  type 00 flour (Caputo Pizzeria or equivalent)
390  g  water (65% hydration)
 17  g  fine sea salt (2.8%)
  1  g  fresh yeast (or 0.3 g instant)
```

1. Dissolve salt in water. Add ~70% of the flour, mix to a slurry. Crumble
   yeast into the slurry. Add remaining flour. Mix to a shaggy dough.
2. Rest 20 min (autolyse). Knead 10–15 min until smooth.
3. Cover and bulk ferment 2 hours at room temperature.
4. Divide and ball into 4 portions of ~250 g. Place into a sealed dough box
   or oiled tray, balls separated.
5. Refrigerate 18–24 hours. Pull out 2 hours before baking; let warm to room
   temperature.
6. Heat your oven (and steel/stone/Ooni) as hot as it goes. For an outdoor
   pizza oven, target 450°C / 850°F on the floor.
7. Stretch one ball into a ~30 cm round by hand (no rolling pin — the pin
   degasses the cornicione). Top with sauce, cheese, basil after bake, oil
   drizzle.
8. Cook 60–90 seconds in a 480°C oven, rotating once.

## If you only have a standard home oven

The closest you'll get to Neapolitan at 550°F:

- Use a baking **steel** (not a stone — steel transfers heat faster) on the
  upper rack.
- Preheat at max temp for **60 minutes**. Don't shortcut this — the steel
  needs time to fully saturate.
- Switch to **broil** for the last 10 minutes of preheat to load the dome
  with heat.
- Stretch your pizza, top it lightly.
- Slide onto the steel under the broiler. Bake 3–4 minutes total, rotating
  halfway.
- Expect: less leopard char, slightly thicker dough texture, more cooked
  centre than true Neapolitan. Still excellent — just not AVPN.

## Why bother with the rules

The AVPN spec is not snobbery. Each rule solves a problem in the system:
00 flour prevents toughness, fresh yeast and long ferment build digestible
gluten and flavour, 480°C ensures the bottom sets in 90 seconds before the
centre dries. Change one variable and the others have to change too.
Understanding why each rule exists is what lets you adapt the style to your
equipment without ruining it.
