# Ovens and equipment — what to buy at every budget

The equipment question matters more in pizza than in almost any other home
cooking, because pizza is a heat-transfer game. The hottest pizzas need the
most extreme equipment.

This document walks through every meaningful tool, from $0 to $3000+, and
which ones move the needle.

## The biggest variable: oven temperature

Most home ovens max at **550°F / 290°C**. Some (especially older European)
max at 250°C / 482°F. A few residential ovens reach 290°C / 555°F.

What that limits:

- **Neapolitan** (requires 750-900°F) → can't truly do at home without an
  outdoor pizza oven.
- **NY / Detroit / Sicilian / Roman / grandma / Chicago** → all bake
  beautifully at 500-550°F.

If you only care about NY and pan-style pizzas, your home oven is fine.
If you want true Neapolitan, you'll need to go outdoor.

## Tier 0 — completely free upgrades

These cost nothing and they all help:

- **Use the oven's broiler.** Most home ovens have a top heating element
  that goes hotter than the bake setting. After the pizza is two-thirds
  done, switching to broil for the last 60-90 seconds can give you better
  cheese browning and char.
- **Position rack high.** Pizza wants top-down heat for cheese browning.
  Upper-middle or upper rack works best.
- **Preheat longer than the manual says.** Pizza wants the entire oven
  cavity, walls included, fully saturated with heat. **60 minutes** is the
  minimum if you have a baking steel; 30 minutes minimum without.
- **Bake on parchment paper for the first 2 minutes**, then slide the
  paper out from under and let the pizza finish directly on the stone /
  steel. This avoids the "launching" problem that ruins many home pies.

## Tier 1 — under $100

### Baking steel ($60-90)

The single biggest home pizza upgrade. A 1/4-inch (or thicker) steel plate
that sits in your oven and absorbs heat for an hour, then transfers it
explosively to the dough when the pizza lands on it.

Why steel beats a baking stone:

- Steel conducts heat **20× faster** than ceramic stone.
- A stone needs longer preheat to fully saturate.
- Steel doesn't crack from thermal shock.
- Steel lasts forever.

Get the **NerdChef Steel Stone** (USA), the **Modernist Cuisine Baking
Steel**, or a generic 1/4-inch hot-rolled steel plate from an industrial
metals supplier (cheapest option, ~$30 if you find a local steel yard).

**A baking steel is the single piece of equipment most home pizzas would
benefit from.** Without one, you're fighting your oven.

### Pizza peel ($15-30)

A flat paddle for launching pizzas onto the hot steel/stone. **Wood is
better than metal for launching** (less sticky); **metal/aluminium is
better for retrieving** (slides under the pizza more easily). Many home
cooks own one of each.

Get a wood "peel" for $15 (cherry or beech) — and consider a **perforated
metal peel** ($20) for retrieving if you bake regularly.

### Bench scraper ($8)

A flat metal scraper for dividing dough and scraping the counter clean.
Useful daily; basically essential.

### Digital scale ($15)

If you don't already have one. Pizza is a recipe-by-weight craft;
measuring by volume gives you inconsistent results.

### Pizza pan or sheet pan ($10-25)

For pan styles: a 9×13 quarter-sheet, a half-sheet, a Detroit Lloyd pan
(see below), or a deep round cake pan for Chicago.

## Tier 2 — $100-300

### Detroit-style pizza pan ($30-50)

If you want to make Detroit-style. **Lloyd Pans** is the industry standard
(10×14 or 8×10). The hard-anodised aluminium gives clean release without
sticking. A generic steel sheet pan works but won't get the same edges.

### Wood pizza peel + metal peel ($30-50 each)

Once you're baking weekly, having both peels separately optimises launching
and retrieving.

### Cast-iron skillet ($30-50)

For grandma-style, bar pies, single-eater pies. A 12-inch cast iron is
versatile and gives a hot, fried bottom. Lodge's classic is fine.

### Pizza screens ($10 each)

Mesh metal rings that hold the pizza shape during baking. Useful for
high-hydration doughs that tear during launching. Some pizzerias bake on
screens; some only use them for the first minute then transfer.

### Better digital thermometer ($20)

Infrared / surface thermometer to read your steel temperature, and an
instant-read for water and dough temp. ThermoWorks makes good ones.

## Tier 3 — $300-1000

### Outdoor pizza oven (entry tier) — Ooni Karu 12, Koda 12, or Roccbox

The breakthrough product category that's transformed home pizza in the
2020s. These are small, portable, propane-fired (or wood) outdoor ovens
that reach **900°F+** in 15-20 minutes.

Top picks at this tier:

- **Ooni Koda 12** ($300, propane only): the home pizza oven for most
  people. Fast preheat, propane convenience, makes excellent 12-inch
  Neapolitan pies.
- **Ooni Karu 12** ($350, wood or propane): adds wood option for
  smoke-flavour devotees. Slightly more setup.
- **Gozney Roccbox** ($500): better-built, slightly larger interior,
  premium feel. Excellent if you'll bake weekly.

**With any of these, you can make true Neapolitan pizza at home.** That's
the difference these ovens make.

### Pizza turning peel ($25)

Small round peel for rotating pizzas inside hot ovens without sliding them
out. Essential for Ooni / Roccbox where pizzas need rotation every 20
seconds.

## Tier 4 — $1000-3000+

### Larger outdoor pizza ovens — Ooni Karu 16, Gozney Dome

- **Ooni Karu 16** ($800-900): fits 16-inch pies. Game-changer for hosting.
- **Gozney Dome** ($2000+): the premium home-installed option. Beautiful,
  large, retains heat well, can do bread and roasts too.

### Wood-fired oven (installed) — Forno Bravo, etc.

A built-in masonry wood-fired oven, professionally installed. $3000-15000
depending on size and finishing. The pinnacle. Beyond the scope of this
guide.

## Buying priority by goal

### Goal: weekly Detroit / pan pizzas at home

1. Baking steel ($60).
2. Detroit Lloyd pan ($45).
3. Wood peel ($15).
4. Cast-iron skillet ($30).

Total ~$150. You can produce excellent pan-style pizzas with this kit.

### Goal: NY / Detroit + occasional Neapolitan

1. Ooni Koda 12 ($300).
2. Baking steel ($60) — for indoor NY-style.
3. Wood peel + metal peel ($30).
4. Detroit Lloyd pan ($45).
5. Pizza turning peel ($25).

Total ~$460. You're now equipped for almost everything.

### Goal: best possible home pizza, hosting

1. Gozney Dome or Roccbox ($500-2000+).
2. Baking steel for indoor versatility.
3. All the small tools.

Total $700-2300+. Diminishing returns above this.

## What NOT to spend on

- **Decorative pizza-shaped stones.** Baking steel beats them at every
  price point.
- **Specialty Italian pizza wheel cutters.** A good kitchen knife cuts pizza
  better than a wheel — the wheel often presses cheese sideways and pulls
  toppings off.
- **Branded "pizza yeast"** sold by certain brands. It's just instant
  yeast with marketing.
- **Pizza-themed peel storage.** A drawer hook costs $2.

## Essential vs nice-to-have

**Truly essential, even for occasional pizza:**

- Baking steel or stone (for any non-pan style)
- Pizza peel (wood)
- Bench scraper
- Digital scale

**Nice-to-have, will improve your results:**

- Surface / infrared thermometer
- Detroit pan (if you do Detroit)
- Cast-iron skillet (versatile)
- Pizza screens (for high-hydration doughs)

**Step-change upgrade, if you're serious:**

- Ooni Koda 12 or equivalent (~$300). Single biggest jump in pie quality
  available to home cooks. Unlocks Neapolitan.

## A meta-rule on equipment

Most home pizza problems are not equipment problems. They are:

- Under-preheated stone/steel.
- Under-fermented dough.
- Over-topped pies.
- Wet sauce ruining the crust.

Fix those before spending money. The baking steel is the one piece of
equipment that genuinely makes everything else easier. Beyond that, the
Ooni is the next leap. Skip the rest.
