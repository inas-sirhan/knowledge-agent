# Other American and niche styles — St. Louis, Greek, California, Trenton tomato pie, New Haven

A short tour of regional American pizzas that don't fit elsewhere. Each is
beloved locally and most are worth at least one attempt at home.

## St. Louis–style

Polarising. The dough is **yeast-free** — leavened with baking powder if at
all — which makes a cracker-thin, almost biscuit-like base. The defining
ingredient is **Provel**, a processed cheese product specific to St. Louis
that's a blend of cheddar, Swiss, and provolone. It melts to a smooth,
gooey, slightly sweet pool — beloved by locals, baffling to most outsiders.
Cut into squares.

If you're making it at home outside St. Louis:

- For Provel, the closest substitute is a 50/50 melted mix of provolone
  and white American cheese (or mozzarella + processed Swiss). It won't be
  exact.
- Roll the dough cracker-thin with a rolling pin.
- Top with a sweetened tomato sauce, then Provel substitute, then bake at
  500°F for ~8 minutes.

## Greek-American pizza (New England "house of pizza")

If you've ever been in New Hampshire or Massachusetts and seen a hundred
"X's House of Pizza" shops, you've seen Greek-American pizza. The style is
defined by:

- Baked in a **well-oiled metal pan** so the bottom **fries** as it bakes.
- A regular yeasted dough (not as thick as Sicilian, not as thin as NY).
- Heavy cheese coverage, often **a blend of cheddar and mozzarella** (the
  cheddar gives the orange tinge characteristic of the style).
- Round, served as a whole pie or slices.
- A slightly herbal, sweet tomato sauce.

Greek immigrants opened pizzerias in New England in the 1950s-60s and
brought their own approach to pizza-making — fried bottoms (from Greek
pita influence), generous cheese, accessible toppings. It's local
comfort food.

For home: a 12-inch oiled round cake pan, your standard NY dough, layer
sauce + cheese + toppings, bake at 500°F for 15 minutes. The fried bottom
is the experience.

## California-style

Originated at **Chez Panisse** (Alice Waters) and **Spago** (Wolfgang Puck)
in the 1980s. The defining trait is **non-traditional toppings** with a
focus on fresh, seasonal, often unexpected ingredients: BBQ chicken, smoked
salmon with crème fraîche, goat cheese with caramelised onions, duck
confit. The base is usually a thin, crisp NY-adjacent dough, sometimes
grilled rather than baked.

It's less a technical style and more a philosophy: pizza as a vehicle for
the season's best produce, with the technical conventions (mozzarella +
tomato + basil) discarded when something better is in season.

For home: any NY or Roman tonda base, then top with whatever's in season —
asparagus + lemon + ricotta in spring, peaches + prosciutto + balsamic in
summer, butternut squash + sage + brown butter in autumn, etc.

## Trenton tomato pie

A Trenton, NJ specialty: **upside-down assembly**. Dough → cheese → tomato
sauce **on top**. (Same inversion as Chicago deep-dish, but on a normal-
thickness pie.) The pies are baked in deck ovens at the legendary
DeLorenzo's and Papa's Tomato Pies (the latter dates to 1912).

Why upside-down? Folklore says it lets the cheese flavour come through
without being masked by sauce. Technically, it also slightly protects the
crust from the sauce moisture.

For home: NY dough, ~13-inch stretched round, low-moisture mozz first
edge-to-edge, then dollops or stripes of well-seasoned reduced tomato
sauce. Bake 500°F on a steel, 5–7 minutes.

## New Haven "apizza" (ah-BEETZ)

A Connecticut tradition centred on **Frank Pepe** and **Sally's** in New
Haven. The style:

- Coal-fired oven (~700°F+), thinner than Neapolitan, with **leopard char
  on the bottom and edges**.
- Oval (not round) shape — a function of how it's stretched.
- **No mozzarella** unless you ask for it (the default is olive oil, garlic,
  oregano, grated Romano, tomato).
- The signature pie is the **white clam pizza** — fresh shucked clams,
  garlic, olive oil, oregano, grated Pecorino, no tomato, no mozz. It's
  one of the great pizzas in America.

The "no cheese unless you ask" thing surprises out-of-towners. New Haven
pizza is a tomato-pie tradition.

For home (approximate): NY dough rolled out long and oval, lightly
brushed with garlic oil, topped with crushed San Marzano + oregano +
grated Romano. Bake on a steel at 550°F for ~5 minutes. For the white
clam: skip the tomato, use shucked clams + garlic + parsley + olive oil
+ Romano. The home oven won't get the coal-char, but the flavour profile
works.

## Cracker-thin frozen pizzas (industrial style)

A separate category, but useful to acknowledge: brands like DiGiorno, Red
Baron, Tony's, and now-discontinued Totino's defined a generation's
understanding of "pizza" in the US. They are usually low-hydration cracker-
thin doughs with binders, par-baked, then flash-frozen. Worth knowing for
context but not really replicable at home.

## When to try these

These styles aren't first-pizza territory. If you've already made
Neapolitan or NY at home and want to deepen your range, dip into:

- **Tomato pie (Trenton or Roman style)** for a tomato-forward break from
  cheesier styles.
- **White pizzas** (New Haven white clam, or a ricotta-based bianca) for a
  cheese-and-aromatics palette that's totally different.
- **California-style** when you have an excellent farmers-market haul and
  want to do it justice.

The point of knowing about them is partly to expand your imagination —
pizza is not one thing.
