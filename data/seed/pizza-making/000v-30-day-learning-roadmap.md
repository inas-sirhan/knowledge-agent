# A 30-day pizza-making learning roadmap

If you want to go from "never made pizza" to "consistently good home pizza"
in a month, this is the structured plan. One pizza session per weekend, four
sessions total. Each session has one new variable to learn.

## Why this plan

Most home-pizza tutorials throw you straight into Neapolitan at 900°F or
72-hour cold ferment NY dough. Both are achievable, but jumping in cold
means you don't isolate what makes a pizza good or bad. This plan introduces
one new variable per week, so you learn the system.

## Week 1 — Grandma pizza, sheet pan, same-day dough

**Goal**: produce a good-looking, good-tasting pizza on your first try
with no special equipment.

### Equipment

- A sheet pan (9×13 or similar).
- Olive oil.
- A digital scale.

### Dough — same-day

```
400 g  bread flour
260 g  water
  8 g  salt
  3 g  instant yeast
 25 g  olive oil
```

1. Mix everything. Knead 5 min by hand.
2. Bulk ferment 90 min at room temp.
3. Press into oiled pan. Proof 30 min.
4. Top with simple sauce + low-moisture mozz + a few pepperoni.
5. Bake at 500°F, middle rack, 18 min.

### What you'll learn

- How a basic pizza dough comes together.
- How to handle it gently (sheet pan = no stretching).
- What 500°F + 18 min produces in your oven.

### What might go wrong (and what to learn from)

- **Pizza tastes bland**: salt or sauce was under-seasoned. Adjust next time.
- **Bottom is pale**: lower rack next time, or longer bake.
- **Crust is dense**: dough was under-proofed. Give it 2 hr next time.

## Week 2 — Same recipe, 24-hour cold ferment

**Goal**: feel the dramatic flavour difference cold fermentation makes,
with no other changes.

### Same recipe as week 1, but:

- Reduce yeast to **1 g** (0.25%) instead of 3 g.
- After kneading, bulk ferment 60 min at room temp.
- **Then refrigerate 24 hours.**
- Pull out 90 min before pressing into pan.
- Everything else identical.

### What you'll learn

- The single biggest flavour upgrade in pizza making.
- How to plan ahead (dough requires a day's lead time).
- Cold-fermented dough's slight tang and improved aroma.

### Cost of admission

You have to plan a day ahead. Worth it.

## Week 3 — NY-style pizza on a baking steel

**Goal**: produce a proper round pizza on a hot stone/steel, with all
the techniques (stretching, launching) that home pizza requires.

### New equipment

- A baking steel ($60-90, the single biggest pizza upgrade you'll make).
- A wood peel ($15) or use parchment paper.

### Dough — 48-hour cold ferment

```
500 g  bread flour
310 g  water (62%)
 12 g  salt
   2 g  instant yeast
 12 g  sugar
 12 g  olive oil
```

1. Mix, knead 8 min.
2. Bulk ferment 60 min at room temp.
3. Divide into 2 × 420 g balls.
4. **Cold ferment 48 hours.**
5. Pull out 90 min before baking.

### Bake setup

- Place baking steel on the **upper-middle rack**.
- Preheat at **maximum oven temp** for **60 minutes**.
- Switch to broil for last 10 min of preheat.

### Stretching

This week's skill challenge:

1. Place dough on a heavily-semolina-dusted counter.
2. Press the centre with fingertips, leaving a 1.5 cm rim untouched.
3. Pick up the dough, drape over knuckles, let gravity stretch it.
4. Continue until ~14 inches across.
5. Transfer to a parchment-dusted peel.

### Toppings

- 80 g cooked tomato sauce (cook a batch with reduced thickness).
- 150 g low-moisture mozz, freshly shredded from a block.
- 40 g small-cup pepperoni.

### Bake

Launch onto the steel using parchment. Bake 6-8 minutes. Rotate at
halfway. Broil last 60 seconds.

### What you'll learn

- The thermal-mass effect of a baking steel (huge difference).
- How to launch a pizza.
- That the same dough, with the same toppings, at high heat with a
  steel gives a different result than a sheet-pan pizza.

## Week 4 — Optimised NY pizza + your style of choice

**Goal**: refine technique on NY-style, OR branch into another style based
on your preferences.

### Option A — Master NY pizza

Use week 3's dough, but adjust based on what went wrong:

- Bottom too pale → longer preheat, lower rack.
- Top burnt → middle rack, shorter broil.
- Crust dense → longer cold ferment (try 72 hours).
- Dough hard to stretch → 1.5-2 hour rest at room temp.

Goal: make 3 pizzas in one session, refining each one. By pizza #3, your
technique should feel automatic.

### Option B — Branch into Detroit

If NY isn't your jam, try Detroit. You already have all the dough skills.

Get a Detroit pan ($45) or use a 9×13 cake pan.

Dough:

```
500 g  bread flour
350 g  water (70%)
 12 g  salt
   3 g  instant yeast
 25 g  oil
```

48-hour cold ferment. Press into oiled pan. Proof 90 min. Top with
cubed mozzarella to the edge of the pan. Bake at 500°F, 14-17 min. Stripe
sauce on top after baking.

### Option C — Branch into Roman al taglio

Try the half-sheet pan rectangle. Different texture entirely. 80%
hydration dough, 72-hour cold ferment, no kneading (just stretch-and-fold).

See the recipe in the canonical-recipes document.

## What you'll have after 30 days

If you follow this plan:

- A proven home dough recipe at two hydrations (62% and 70%).
- Comfort with a 48-72 hour cold ferment workflow.
- Stretching technique that doesn't tear dough.
- Familiarity with your specific oven's quirks.
- A baking steel that paid for itself in pizza nights.
- A consistent process for sauce.

This is the floor. Above this floor, you can experiment freely.

## Week 5+ — where to go next

Once the basics are reliable, branch in any direction:

- **Get an outdoor pizza oven** (Ooni Koda 12 ~$300) and unlock true
  Neapolitan.
- **Experiment with pre-ferments** (poolish, biga) for more flavour
  without changing your timeline.
- **Try grilled pizza** for outdoor summer evenings.
- **Try other styles** from the styles documents in this KB.
- **Build a sourdough starter** if you want a deeper flavour project.

## The big lesson

Pizza is iterative. Each batch teaches you something the previous one
couldn't. **Take notes** every time:

- Recipe used (flour %, hydration, yeast %).
- Ferment time + temperature.
- Bake details (oven temp, rack, time, rotation timing).
- What went well.
- What went wrong.
- What you'd change.

A small notebook or a single text file is enough. After 10 pizzas, you'll
have personal data on what works for your kitchen and your tastes. After
20, you'll be ahead of most home cooks who've made pizza for years
without tracking variables.

The shortcut to fast improvement isn't more recipes. It's the same recipe,
made repeatedly, with one variable changed each time, and notes on what
the change did.
