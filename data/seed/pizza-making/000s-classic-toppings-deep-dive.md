# A topping-by-topping deep dive

Every classic pizza topping has technique to it. This document goes through
the most common toppings in detail — how to source, prep, apply, and
combine them.

## Pepperoni

The single most popular pizza topping in America.

### What it is

A cured, dried pork-and-beef sausage seasoned with paprika and chili.
Italian-American (not actually Italian — in Italy, "peperoni" means bell
peppers). The deep red colour is from paprika.

### Two important categories

- **Standard deli pepperoni**: ~4-5 cm diameter, sliced thin (1-2 mm).
  Stays flat in the oven. Most supermarket pepperoni.
- **Small-cup pepperoni** ("cup-and-char" / "old-world"): ~3 cm diameter,
  natural casing or formulated to cup. Brands: Ezzo, Battistoni, Hormel
  Rosa Grande, Vermont Smoke and Cure. The pizzeria choice.

The cup-and-char variety curls into bowls in the oven, filling with
rendered fat, then chars at the edges. The textural and flavour
difference is significant.

### How to apply

- **On top of cheese** for NY-style — the pepperoni gets the most
  direct heat from the oven.
- **Underneath cheese** in some styles (Detroit) — different effect, more
  flavour-imparting fat saturates the cheese.
- **In a single layer**, not piled.
- **Don't overlap**. Each slice needs space to cup.

### Quantity

A 12-inch NY pizza takes ~50-70 g of pepperoni. About 25-35 slices for
standard, 15-20 for small-cup (which are smaller diameter and provide
more flavour per slice).

## Sausage

Italian sausage on pizza is one of the great American food combinations.

### Types

- **Sweet Italian sausage**: with fennel, no heat.
- **Hot Italian sausage**: with crushed red pepper.
- **Fennel sausage**: emphasized fennel, mild.

### How to apply

Remove from casing if buying as links. Break into small clumps with your
fingers — **don't ball them up**, small irregular clumps cook more evenly
and distribute better.

**Apply raw** to pizzas with longer bake times (NY at 6+ min, Detroit at
14+ min). The bake cooks the sausage through.

**Pre-cook** for very high-heat ovens (Neapolitan at 90 seconds) — the
sausage won't fully cook in that time.

Drain rendered fat from pre-cooked sausage so it doesn't make the pizza
greasy.

## Mushrooms

The most-abused topping. Wet, poorly applied raw mushrooms ruin many
home pizzas.

### Two approaches

**Approach A — raw, sliced thin:**

- Slice mushrooms 3-4 mm thick (button or cremini).
- Apply on top of cheese.
- Works for NY-style at 500-550°F where there's enough bake time to drive
  off mushroom moisture.

**Approach B — pre-cooked (better for most cases):**

- Slice mushrooms.
- Sauté in butter or olive oil with a pinch of salt, medium-high heat,
  until they've released their water and re-absorbed it (5-7 min).
- Cool, then apply.
- Works for any style. Highly recommended for high-hydration / wet-prone
  pizzas.

### Mushroom varieties beyond button

- **Cremini** (baby bella): same family as button, slightly more flavour.
- **Shiitake**: chewier, more savory. Pre-cook is essential — they release
  a lot of water.
- **Oyster mushrooms**: delicate, sauté lightly.
- **Wild mushrooms** (chanterelle, morel, porcini): luxury topping. Sauté
  with garlic and thyme; use sparingly because of cost and intensity.

## Onions

### Two approaches

**Raw thin slices:** Slice paper-thin (use a mandolin if you have one).
Apply on top of cheese. The thin slices cook through in 6 minutes; they're
sharp and oniony.

**Caramelized:** Slow-cook sliced onions with a pinch of salt in olive oil
or butter, low heat, 25-40 minutes, until deep golden and sweet. A
spoonful of caramelized onion transforms a pizza. Especially great with
white pies, gorgonzola, or smoked mozzarella.

**Sautéed sweet onions** (Vidalia, sweet white) — softened in oil but not
caramelized. Good middle ground.

## Peppers

Red, green, or yellow bell peppers.

### Approach

- **Slice thin** (mandolin or sharp knife).
- **Pre-cook briefly** if your bake is short, or apply raw if it's a
  longer bake.
- For maximum flavour: **roast peppers** before using. Char in a 500°F
  oven 20 min, peel, slice. Sweeter, more concentrated.
- Pickled peppers (Calabrian chiles, banana peppers, pepperoncini) bring
  acidity and don't need pre-cooking.

## Olives

- **Kalamata** (purple-black, Greek): briny, intense.
- **Black olives, "California black"** (the canned mild ones): much
  milder, almost neutral.
- **Castelvetrano** (bright green Sicilian): buttery, mild. Excellent on
  pizza.
- **Niçoise**: small, tart, wine-flavoured. Good on white pies.

Halve or slice olives. Drain well. Don't overdo — they're salty and bold.

## Anchovies

The most polarising topping. Done right, they're savory and almost
melt-into-the-pizza unrecognisable. Done wrong, they're hairy and
overwhelmingly salty.

### How to use them

- Use **good anchovies**: oil-packed, from Spain or Italy (Ortiz,
  Roland's Premium, Recca). Avoid the cheap salt-packed in cans.
- **Place under cheese**. The cheese protects them from drying out and
  they dissolve into the dish.
- Use **2-4 fillets per 12-inch pizza** — they're strongly flavoured.
- Pair with strong companions: capers, olives, garlic, oregano.

## Basil

- **Always after the bake**. High heat destroys basil.
- **Tear** rather than slice — slicing browns the leaf.
- A drizzle of olive oil over the basil after locks in flavour.

## Garlic

The trickiest pantry ingredient on a hot pizza.

### The problem

Fresh garlic in a 500°F oven burns within 2 minutes, turning bitter and
acrid.

### Solutions

- **Garlic confit**: slow-cook peeled garlic cloves in olive oil at low
  heat (180°F / 80°C) for 1 hour. Soft, sweet, pre-cooked. Spread on
  dough or dot in spoonfuls. Lasts 2 weeks in the fridge.
- **Roasted garlic**: roast a head of garlic at 350°F for 40 min wrapped
  in foil with olive oil. Squeeze the soft cloves out. Use as a base on
  white pies.
- **Garlic-infused oil**: gently warm olive oil with crushed garlic on
  low heat for 15 min. Strain. Brush onto pizza dough before topping.
- **Minced raw garlic in oil**: mix finely minced raw garlic with olive
  oil 1:1, brush onto the dough. The oil insulates the garlic from
  burning.

**Don't** sprinkle raw chopped garlic directly onto a hot pizza. It
burns.

## Tomato — fresh

Different from sauce. Fresh tomatoes (cherry, sliced Roma, or sliced
beefsteak in summer) can be a beautiful topping.

### Approach

- **Salt them 15 min before** using and let them drain. Excess water is
  the enemy.
- Apply on top of cheese, often combined with fresh mozzarella for a
  Margherita-adjacent pie.
- Best in **summer** when tomatoes are at peak — out of season, use
  cherry tomatoes (more consistent).

## Cured meats added after the bake

The Italian charcuterie technique: bake the pizza plain or lightly topped,
then drape thin slices of cured meat over the hot pie. The residual heat
warms the meat without cooking out its delicacy.

Works beautifully with:

- **Prosciutto crudo**: drape on a pie out of the oven. Optionally add
  arugula tossed with lemon.
- **Bresaola**: cured beef, beautiful on a pie with arugula and
  parmigiano.
- **Speck**: smoky alpine prosciutto. Pairs with mushroom or potato pies.
- **Mortadella**: pair with pistachio cream or burrata. A Roman al
  taglio classic.

## Eggs

- **Cracked on top before the final minute or two of bake**, in a small
  well of cheese to keep it from running off. Pull the pizza when the
  white is set and the yolk is still runny.
- Great on white pies, pizza bianca, breakfast-style pies.

## Hot honey

The modern American invention. A spicy-sweet honey (Mike's Hot Honey is
the standard brand) drizzled on a pizza, usually pepperoni, after baking.
The combination of crispy meat + salty cheese + sweet heat is
remarkable.

DIY: warm honey with crushed red pepper flakes (or sliced fresh chili),
infuse 30 min, strain.

## Truffle (or truffle oil)

The most divisive topping in fancy pizzerias.

- **Real fresh truffle**: shaved over a hot pie after the bake. Pricey
  but unforgettable.
- **Truffle oil**: most truffle oil contains synthetic 2,4-dithiapentane
  rather than real truffle. Some hate it; some love it. If you use it,
  use it after the bake (high heat destroys the aroma) and use sparingly.

## Pineapple (yes, we're going there)

The most-argued topping in pizza culture. The reality:

- **It works** flavour-wise. Sweet + salty + savoury is a real culinary
  pattern.
- **It's polarising** culturally. Some people will hate it on principle.
- **If you do it**: pat dry well (pineapple is wet), apply on top of
  cheese, often paired with ham or bacon (Hawaiian).
- **Eat what you want.** Pizza is your dinner.

## Combinations that work

A few canonical American combinations:

- **Sausage + mushroom + onion**: the classic.
- **Pepperoni + jalapeño**: easy upgrade to standard pepperoni.
- **Mushroom + spinach + ricotta**: vegetarian, rich, satisfying.
- **Prosciutto + arugula + parmigiano + lemon oil**: post-bake assembly.
- **Caramelized onion + gorgonzola + walnut + thyme**: a sophisticated
  white pizza.
- **Mortadella + pistachio cream + burrata**: Roman al taglio greatest hit.
- **Pepperoni + hot honey**: modern American classic.

## Combinations that don't

- **Pineapple + anchovy**: too much sweet-salt-funk competition.
- **5+ toppings of any kind**: too much for the structure.
- **Two strong-flavoured cheeses on the same pie**: pick one to lead.
- **Raw garlic + delicate basil**: garlic dominates everything.

The rule of three: most pizzas are at their best with three or fewer
toppings beyond cheese and sauce. Restraint reads as confidence.
