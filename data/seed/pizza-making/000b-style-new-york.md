# New York–style pizza

If Neapolitan is "the original," New York pizza is the **adaptation** — the
form that emerged when Neapolitan immigrants tried to make their pies in
American gas and deck ovens, with American flour, in slice-shop economics
that demanded a larger, more durable pie. It's arguably the most influential
pizza style in the world: the global mental image of "a slice of pizza" is
overwhelmingly NY.

## What it actually is

- **Large** (~45–50 cm / 18–20 inch diameter) and **thin** but with
  structural integrity — a slice should fold lengthwise without tearing.
- **Crust**: crisp on the bottom, pliable on top, with a thin rim (not the
  pillowy cornicione of Neapolitan).
- **Cheese**: low-moisture mozzarella, distributed edge to edge, dense
  enough to brown lightly.
- **Sauce**: cooked, slightly sweet, well-seasoned — different from
  Neapolitan's raw tomato approach.
- **Toppings**: more generous than Neapolitan, and pepperoni dominates the
  American imagination.

The signature move is the **fold** — you fold a slice lengthwise into a
"U" shape, which (a) keeps the toppings on, (b) lets you eat it walking,
and (c) reveals whether your slice is correct (good NY pizza folds without
cracking).

## Dough — the chemistry

NY dough differs from Neapolitan in four key ways:

1. **Higher-protein flour** — bread flour or "high-gluten" flour (King
   Arthur Bread Flour at ~12.7% protein, or All Trumps at ~14%). This
   creates more structure for the larger, thinner pie.
2. **Lower hydration** — typically 60–65% (Neapolitan is 60–65% too; the
   difference is more in the flour).
3. **A small amount of oil and sugar** are usually added. Sugar feeds the
   yeast and helps with browning at lower oven temperatures. Oil tenderises
   the crumb and aids the crispy bottom.
4. **Longer cold ferment** — 48–72 hours in the fridge is the norm. This is
   where NY pizza develops its complex flavour. Same-day NY dough exists but
   the difference is real.

A canonical NY dough (4 pizzas, ~430 g each = enough for ~18-inch pies):

```
1000 g  bread flour or high-gluten flour
 620 g  water (62% hydration)
  25 g  fine sea salt (2.5%)
   3 g  instant yeast (0.3%)
  25 g  sugar (2.5%)
  25 g  olive oil (2.5%)
```

1. Mix water, sugar, salt, yeast briefly. Add flour and oil; mix to shaggy
   dough.
2. Rest 20 min, knead 10 min.
3. Divide into 4 balls (~430 g each).
4. Refrigerate 48–72 hours.
5. Pull out 2 hours before baking; let come to room temperature.

## Oven and the cook

NY pizza is built for a different heat curve than Neapolitan. NY deck ovens
run at **600–650°F / 315–340°C**, and pies bake for **5–7 minutes**. That's
slow enough to fully cook the larger pie and develop the crisp bottom, but
hot enough to brown the cheese.

For a home oven (max 550°F / 290°C):

1. Baking **steel** on the upper-middle rack.
2. **Preheat 60 minutes at max temp.** This is non-negotiable. The steel
   needs to saturate.
3. Stretch your dough to 14–15 inches (a full 18 won't fit most home ovens
   on a steel; that's fine).
4. Top: sauce edge-to-edge with a 1 cm border, low-moisture mozzarella,
   pepperoni or whatever toppings.
5. Slide onto the steel. Bake 6–8 minutes (your oven will vary). Rotate at
   halfway.
6. The bottom should be brown and crisp; the cheese lightly browned.

## Cheese and sauce — done the NY way

- **Cheese**: **low-moisture mozzarella, shredded.** Galbani Whole Milk is a
  widely-available standard; Polly-O and Grande are commercial-grade staples.
  Avoid fresh mozz (too wet) and avoid pre-shredded (anti-caking agents
  prevent it from melting smoothly).
- **Sauce**: a **cooked** tomato sauce. Crush whole peeled tomatoes (San
  Marzano or quality Californian), simmer 15–20 minutes with garlic, dried
  oregano, salt, sugar, and olive oil. Some shops add a touch of grated
  Romano. The goal is a thick, savoury, slightly sweet sauce that won't
  wet the dough.

```
1 can (28 oz) whole peeled tomatoes, crushed by hand
2 cloves garlic, minced
1 tsp dried oregano
1 tsp salt
1 tsp sugar
2 tbsp olive oil
```

Simmer uncovered 15–20 min, reducing to a thick consistency. Cool before use.

## Toppings — the NY repertoire

- **Pepperoni** (the most iconic). Use small-diameter pepperoni (~3 cm)
  with high beef tallow content — these "cup and char" in the oven,
  creating little dishes of rendered fat that crisp.
- **Sausage** (broken-up Italian sweet sausage, raw, placed on top).
- **Mushrooms** (sliced thin, raw — they'll cook in 6 minutes).
- **White (no sauce)** — ricotta + mozzarella + garlic + olive oil.
- **Margherita** — fresh mozz, basil, lighter sauce. (Technically Neapolitan
  but NY pizzerias serve it.)
- **Hawaiian, BBQ chicken, buffalo chicken** — these belong to the
  "American pizzeria" repertoire that emerged in the 1980s-90s.

## A note on slicing and serving

NY pizza is cut into **8 large triangular slices** from a single 18-inch
pie. You can buy "a slice" anywhere in New York — slice shops survive on
the economics of selling cheap individual slices and reheating on demand. A
freshly-baked slice is different from a reheated one. Both are legitimate.

## How to know yours came out right

- The slice folds lengthwise without cracking. The bottom is crisp but
  pliable, browned with a slight char in spots.
- Cheese is melted and lightly browned, with edges that have set without
  burning.
- Pepperoni (if used) has cup-and-charred.
- The crust rim is thin (1–1.5 cm) — not the pillow of Neapolitan.

## Common NY mistakes at home

- **Pre-shredded cheese.** It contains cellulose to prevent caking, and it
  ruins the melt. Buy a block, shred it yourself.
- **Wet sauce.** If it pools on top of the cheese, your sauce wasn't
  reduced enough. Simmer it longer next time.
- **Stretching too thin.** A 14-inch pie should still have a thin rim and
  a structural floor. If you can see the steel through the dough, you've
  over-stretched.
- **No preheat.** A cold steel = soggy bottom. 60 minutes minimum.
- **Cold dough.** Cold dough won't stretch — it'll spring back and tear.
  Pull from fridge 2 hours before.

## Why NY pizza works

NY pizza is the answer to a specific engineering problem: how do you make a
large, transportable, slice-able, well-flavoured pie in a deck oven at
600°F? The answer involves higher-protein flour (for structure at size),
some oil and sugar (for browning at lower heat), longer cold ferment (for
flavour), thick reduced sauce (so the bottom stays crisp), and low-moisture
mozz (so the top doesn't pool water). Every detail is connected. Skip the
cold ferment and you get bland dough; pre-shred your cheese and you get
gluey melt; under-preheat your steel and you get soggy bottom. Get them all
right and you have one of the great food forms.
