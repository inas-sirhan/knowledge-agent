# Vegan and gluten-free pizza — substitutions that actually work

Pizza is one of the easier "I have a dietary restriction" foods because the
dough and the toppings are independent of each other. This document goes
through the substitutions that work and the ones that don't.

## Vegan pizza

### The dough — almost always vegan already

Most pizza doughs are already vegan: flour, water, salt, yeast, and
olive oil. **No dairy, no eggs.**

Exceptions:

- Some Chicago deep-dish recipes use butter — substitute with vegan butter
  (Earth Balance, Miyoko's) or olive oil 1:1.
- Some American "buttery" Sicilian recipes add milk or cream — substitute
  with oat milk or unsweetened almond milk.

Verify by reading the recipe carefully. The Neapolitan, NY, Detroit,
Sicilian, Roman al taglio, and grandma recipes in this knowledge base are
all naturally vegan.

### Cheese — the hard problem

Vegan cheese is the part that's improved dramatically in the last 5 years
but is still not a 1:1 replacement.

#### Good vegan cheese options

- **Violife** (block, NOT the slices — the slices won't melt well).
  Shreds, melts, browns acceptably. Closest to mozzarella behaviour.
- **Miyoko's Mozzarella**: fresh-style. Softer melt, more "fresh mozz"
  texture. Best for Neapolitan-style.
- **Daiya Mozzarella Style Shreds**: melts but doesn't brown well. OK
  for pan styles where you don't need the browning.

For Detroit specifically: use Violife shredded blocks. The frico edge
won't be the same (no real fat fries against the pan), but it gets close.

#### Cheese-replacement strategies

Sometimes the answer isn't a one-to-one cheese substitute, but a redesigned
topping:

- **Cashew cream**: blend soaked cashews with water, nutritional yeast,
  garlic, lemon juice, salt → ricotta-like white pizza topping. Excellent
  on white pies.
- **Hummus + olive oil**: surprisingly works as a base on a Neapolitan-
  style dough with vegetables.
- **Garlic confit + olive oil**: bare-bones but rich. Roman flatbread
  approach.
- **Pesto** (vegan pesto, no parmesan): topping in itself.

### Toppings — the easy part

Most pizza toppings are already vegan:

- **Vegetables**: peppers, mushrooms, onions, olives, artichoke hearts,
  spinach, arugula, basil, sun-dried tomatoes, capers, garlic, etc.
- **Vegan meats**: plant-based pepperoni (Field Roast, Lightlife), vegan
  sausage (Beyond Sausage works great crumbled).
- **Marinated tofu** as a "cheese-adjacent" protein.

### A great fully vegan pizza recipe

```
NY-style dough (already vegan)
NY sauce (already vegan)
Topping per 12-inch pie:
  - 150 g Violife mozzarella shreds
  - 1 cup roasted vegetables (peppers, onions, mushrooms — pre-roast for
    moisture management)
  - 1/2 cup crumbled vegan sausage, pre-browned
  - Fresh basil and a drizzle of olive oil after bake
```

Bake at 500°F on a steel for 7 minutes. Add basil and oil after.

### Vegan Margherita (the harder one)

A traditional Margherita is hard to replicate vegan because the fresh
mozzarella is half the dish. Best approximation:

- Use **Miyoko's Mozzarella Fresh** in small dollops.
- Or use **cashew ricotta** (blend cashews + lemon + salt + a tiny bit of
  white miso for funk).
- Generous fresh basil and excellent olive oil after the bake.

It's not the same as a traditional Margherita. It's a good pizza on its own
terms. Manage expectations.

## Gluten-free pizza

Harder than vegan. Gluten is what makes pizza dough... pizza dough. Without
gluten, you're making something fundamentally different — a flatbread base.

### The right expectations

A gluten-free pizza is not going to fool anyone who's tasted regular pizza.
The crust will be:

- Denser (no gas bubbles trapped by gluten network).
- More crumbly / less chewy.
- Often slightly grainy from alternative flours.

But it can be **delicious in its own right**, especially the right styles.

### What works well — pan styles

Detroit-style is the best gluten-free target. Why:

- The pan does the structural work that gluten normally provides.
- High oil content carries flavour.
- The thick crumb hides the textural difference.
- The frico edge gives a sensory highlight that distracts from crust
  differences.

NY-style and Neapolitan are the hardest to do gluten-free — the thin
crust + handling demands really do require gluten.

### Flour mixes

A few approaches:

#### Pre-made gluten-free pizza flour blends

- **Caputo Fioreglut**: Italian-made gluten-free flour, the standard for
  serious gluten-free pizza. Pricier but reliable.
- **King Arthur Gluten-Free Measure for Measure**: convenient, widely
  available.
- **Bob's Red Mill Gluten-Free Pizza Crust Mix**: works for basic results.

Use these per their package instructions. They contain xanthan gum or
psyllium husk (which simulate the elasticity gluten provides).

#### Homemade gluten-free flour blend

For experienced bakers:

```
200 g  rice flour (or 100 g brown rice + 100 g white rice)
 50 g  potato starch
 30 g  tapioca starch
 10 g  xanthan gum
```

Use in a recipe with the same hydration as regular pizza dough. Add 1 tsp
psyllium husk for better stretch. Mix and rest 1 hour before shaping.

#### Naturally gluten-free flours

- **Chickpea flour**: makes a dense, savoury, slightly Indian-tasting pizza
  base. Good for grandma-style with bold tomato.
- **Buckwheat flour**: nutty, dark. Good with mushrooms and arugula.
- **Cauliflower crust**: not actually flour — riced cauliflower + egg +
  cheese baked into a "crust." Works but is its own category of food.

### Gluten-free dough technique

The dough won't behave like wheat dough. You can't develop gluten because
there is none. Approach:

1. **Don't knead** — there's nothing to develop.
2. **Mix briefly** until evenly combined.
3. **Rest 30-60 minutes** to hydrate the flours.
4. **Press**, don't stretch — gluten-free dough doesn't stretch.
5. **Use parchment** under the dough during shaping; it's too fragile to
   move without support.
6. **Bake slightly longer** than wheat pizza — gluten-free dough takes
   longer to set in the middle.

### A canonical gluten-free pizza

```
500 g  Caputo Fioreglut (or similar GF blend)
400 g  water (80% — gluten-free flours absorb more)
 10 g  salt
  5 g  instant yeast
 25 g  olive oil
```

1. Mix all ingredients to a thick batter.
2. Rest 30 min.
3. Spread onto a parchment-lined baking steel or pan with a spatula —
   it'll be wetter than wheat dough.
4. Par-bake at 500°F for 5 minutes (this sets the crust).
5. Remove, top, return to oven for another 6-8 minutes.

### Gluten-free for someone who eats both

If you live with one gluten-free person and several gluten-eaters,
practical tips:

- Make their pizza FIRST so the steel doesn't have wheat crumbs on it.
- Or dedicate a parchment-lined pan to the gluten-free pie.
- Don't share spatulas or peels between wheat and gluten-free pies — wash
  thoroughly between.

## Vegan AND gluten-free

It's possible. Use:

- A gluten-free dough (above).
- Vegan cheese (Violife shreds melt best for thicker crusts).
- Plant-based toppings.

The result will be the most heavily-substituted pizza in the kitchen, and
quality will suffer. But it's edible and the right pizza for someone with
both restrictions. Lean into Detroit-style for best results.

## A few other diet adjustments

### Keto / low-carb

- **Fathead dough**: mozzarella + cream cheese + almond flour + egg.
  Surprisingly pizza-like, much lower carb than regular dough. The blueprint
  recipe is widely available.
- **Cauliflower crust**: ~10g net carbs per pie.

### Low-sodium

- Reduce sauce salt to 1/2 tsp per can.
- Skip pecorino topping.
- Use low-sodium cheese (look for fresh mozzarella, which is much lower
  sodium than aged or low-moisture mozz).

### High-protein

- Add cooked chicken or grilled shrimp as toppings.
- Use a higher-protein flour for the dough.
- Increase cheese (mozz is 5 g protein per 30 g).
