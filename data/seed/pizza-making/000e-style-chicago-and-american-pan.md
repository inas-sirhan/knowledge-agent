# Chicago and other American pan pizzas — deep dish, tavern, grandma, bar pies

A grab-bag of distinctively American pan-baked styles that don't fit neatly
into NY or Detroit. Each is its own engineering solution and has its own
local culture.

## Chicago deep-dish

The dish your out-of-town friends think Chicagoans eat. They mostly don't.
But it's an iconic dish on its own terms and worth understanding.

### Identity

- Baked in a **deep round pan**, 5–7 cm tall.
- The order of assembly is **inverted**: dough on bottom and up the sides
  → cheese (often thick slices of mozzarella laid directly on dough) →
  toppings (sausage, pepperoni) → chunky tomato sauce **on top** → grated
  parmesan.
- The cheese-on-bottom protects the dough from the wet sauce on top.
- Cooking time is **30–45 minutes** because the pie is so deep.
- It's a casserole, structurally and conceptually.

### Dough

Chicago deep-dish dough is unusual: it has cornmeal or semolina for texture,
sometimes butter, and a relatively low hydration. It's biscuit-adjacent —
less elastic than a typical pizza dough.

```
400 g  all-purpose flour
 60 g  fine cornmeal
240 g  water (60% hydration)
 10 g  salt
  4 g  instant yeast
 30 g  olive oil
 30 g  melted butter (optional, makes it richer)
```

1. Mix, knead lightly, bulk ferment 60 min.
2. Press into a generously oiled 9-inch deep dish pan, dough up the sides.
3. Proof in pan 30 min.
4. Layer cheese (thick slices, ~250 g), then toppings, then chunky uncooked
   crushed tomatoes seasoned with salt, sugar, garlic, oregano, then grated
   parmesan.
5. Bake at 425°F / 220°C, **lower rack**, 30–35 min. The crust should be
   deeply golden, the top should be bubbling but not burnt.

### Toppings

- **Italian sausage** is the most traditional topping — often as a layer
  of raw sausage broken up over the cheese.
- Pepperoni, mushrooms, peppers, onions.
- Some Chicagoans add an additional thin layer of "stuffed" cheese on top
  before the sauce — that's "stuffed pizza" (a related variant from Giordano's
  and others).

### Why it works

The deep pan creates a different food than a regular pizza — it's
structurally a savoury pie. The cheese-bottom-then-sauce-on-top inversion
solves the soggy-bottom problem: the cheese acts as a moisture barrier and
the sauce gets to caramelise lightly on the top exposed to oven heat.

## Chicago tavern / thin-crust ("party cut")

What Chicagoans **actually** eat day to day. Often forgotten outside the
Midwest.

### Identity

- **Cracker-thin** crust (no rise — usually a low-yeast or no-yeast dough
  rolled paper-thin).
- Round but **cut into squares** ("party cut" or "tavern cut") — small
  enough that everyone gets a piece.
- Toppings (sausage especially) spread edge-to-edge.
- Mozzarella, light tomato sauce, dried oregano, no fancy ingredients.

### Dough

```
400 g  all-purpose flour
 220 g  water (55% hydration, low)
   8 g  salt
   1 g  instant yeast
  20 g  oil
```

1. Mix and knead briefly. Rest 60 min (no big rise needed).
2. **Roll thin with a rolling pin** (this is essential — no stretching, no
   degassing concerns; we want it cracker-thin).
3. Move to a parchment-lined sheet or perforated round pan.
4. Top sparingly: sauce, cheese to edge, sausage / pepperoni / mushrooms.
5. Bake 500°F / 260°C, ~8–10 min.

The result is light, crunchy, and unmistakably Midwestern. It pairs with
Old Style beer in a wood-panelled tavern. That's the brief.

## Grandma pizza — the Long Island home pan style

Started as a home cook's solution: when you don't have a wood-fired oven
or a deck oven, you bake a pan pizza. Grandma pizza is the gift of that
constraint to the world.

### Identity

- Thin (but not cracker-thin), baked in a rectangular sheet pan.
- A heavy crust of olive oil on the pan creates a fried, crispy bottom.
- Sauce-cheese-(toppings) layering, but the sauce is often applied **after**
  a brief par-bake or in stripes.
- The signature is **garlic, dried oregano, and pecorino** dusted on top.

### Dough

```
400 g  bread flour
260 g  water (65% hydration)
  8 g  salt
  3 g  instant yeast
 25 g  olive oil
```

1. Mix, knead 5 min, bulk 60 min, cold ferment 24 hr (optional, much
   better).
2. Press into an oiled 13×9 (or 9×13) sheet pan.
3. Proof 60 min.
4. Top: thin layer of crushed fresh tomato (uncooked, salted, with garlic
   and oregano), thin layer of low-moisture mozzarella, drizzle of oil,
   sprinkle of pecorino.
5. Bake 500°F / 260°C, lower rack, 15–18 min.

### Why people love grandma

Easier than NY round pizza (no stretch). More tomato-forward than Detroit
(the chopped fresh tomato is the headliner). And the bottom gets a slightly
fried, crisp character from the oil that's hard to achieve any other way.
It's a great weeknight pizza.

## Bar pies (NJ, NYC)

A small, personal-sized round pizza (8–10 inches) baked in a deck oven and
served whole — meant to be eaten with beer at a bar.

- Very thin, crisp underneath, slightly charred.
- One per person.
- Toppings are sparse but bold (good pepperoni, fresh tomato, generous
  olive oil).
- The style is associated with North Jersey institutions like Star Tavern
  (Orange, NJ), where bar pies are a religion.

For home cooks: bar pie is essentially "NY pizza, but smaller and crispier".
Stretch your NY dough into an 8-inch round, top lightly, bake on a hot
steel for 5 minutes. You'll get something close.

## Picking among them

| You want | Pick |
|---|---|
| A casserole-pizza, eat-with-a-fork, impressive | Chicago deep-dish |
| A weeknight family pizza that's actually feasible | Grandma or tavern |
| A crisp, thin slice the way Chicagoans eat it | Tavern |
| A single-eater crisp pie with beer | Bar pie |
| A learning project with low risk | Grandma |

## Why pan pizzas are great for home

All five of these styles bake at standard 500°F home-oven temperatures with
no special equipment beyond a sheet pan, a baking steel, or a cast-iron
skillet. The pan handles the structural challenge. The dough is forgiving.
You don't have to learn to launch a stretched round off a peel. If you're
intimidated by NY or Neapolitan, start here.
