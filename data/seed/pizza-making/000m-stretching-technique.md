# Stretching technique — the most-failed step in home pizza

The dough is fermented. The oven is hot. And then most home pizzas fail at
**the stretch**. The crust ends up uneven, torn, or springs back to a thick
disc. This is a skill that takes 5-10 pies to develop and a few minutes to
explain.

## Goal

A round (or rectangular) piece of dough, **even thickness across the middle**
(2-3 mm for thin styles, 5-6 mm for thick), with a slightly thicker rim
that will puff into a cornicione. **No tears.** **No flour in the middle**
(flour on the bottom of the dough will burn on a hot stone).

## Setup

1. Pull dough from the fridge **1.5-2 hours before** you intend to stretch.
   Cold dough is impossible to stretch without springing back.
2. Have a clean, lightly floured surface (use semolina — it doesn't burn).
3. Have your peel ready, dusted with semolina.
4. Have toppings prepped and at the counter. Once the dough is stretched,
   you have 60-90 seconds before it sticks to the peel.

## The hand stretch — round pizzas

The traditional Neapolitan / NY method. **No rolling pin.**

1. **Press the centre** of the dough ball with your fingertips, leaving the
   outer ring (1-2 cm wide) untouched. This pushes air toward the rim,
   which is what becomes the cornicione.
2. **Continue pressing**, working outward in a spiral, until you have a
   flat disc with a slightly puffed rim. The disc is now ~15 cm wide.
3. **Pick up the dough.** Drape it over your knuckles (knuckles up, thumb
   curled inside) and let gravity pull it down.
4. **Rotate your knuckles**, slowly turning the dough so different sections
   get pulled. Gravity does most of the work.
5. Alternative: lay the dough flat and use a "slap and stretch" — pick
   up an edge, slap it down a few cm away. Rotate. Repeat. The dough
   stretches as the floppy edge slaps.
6. When the dough is ~30 cm and feels right, transfer to a semolina-dusted
   peel for topping.

Common errors:

- **Pressing into the rim.** This degasses the cornicione. Press centre
  only.
- **Using a rolling pin.** Pin = degassed rim = flat cornicione.
- **Stretching too fast.** Aggressive stretches tear; gentle gravity
  doesn't.
- **Trying to stretch cold dough.** Won't work; will spring back.

## The pan stretch — Detroit, Sicilian, grandma

Easier than round-pizza stretching because the pan does the structural work.

1. Coat the pan generously with olive oil.
2. Plop the dough ball into the pan.
3. With oiled hands, gently press the dough toward the corners. Some
   resistance is normal — don't fight it.
4. Cover the pan. Let the dough rest 20-30 min. The gluten will relax.
5. Press again — this time the dough will reach the corners.
6. If after 60 min the dough still hasn't fully filled the pan, that's
   fine. It will continue to relax during the final proof. Keep gently
   redistributing every 30 min.

## The al taglio stretch — long rectangles

Different from pan stretch because al taglio dough is wet and sticky.

1. **Heavy semolina on the counter** — don't be shy.
2. Tip the dough out of its container. Don't deflate it deliberately —
   you want to preserve the gas bubbles.
3. Gently lift and stretch the dough by holding two opposite corners,
   pulling them apart with controlled tension.
4. Rotate, repeat from the other corners.
5. Once the dough is roughly rectangular and slightly larger than your
   half-sheet pan, transfer it to the pan.
6. Inside the pan: dimple with **fingertips**, not knuckles. The dimples
   are characteristic of al taglio.

## What "stretch enough but not too much" feels like

- Hold the dough up to a light. The middle should be **translucent** —
  you should see the warm glow of a window or lamp through it.
- Resist the urge to push it to "see-through." Once you can see your
  fingers through it, you've gone too thin.

## The dough won't stretch — diagnostic

- **Springs back hard, won't budge.**
  → Dough is cold. Let it sit another 30-45 min.
  → Dough is under-fermented. Let it sit 1-2 more hours.
- **Tears as you stretch.**
  → Over-fermented (gluten broken down). Salvageable — work gently, use
    the dough for grandma-style where tears can be patched in the pan.
- **Stretches but the middle is thicker than the edges.**
  → You pressed into the rim. Re-stretch and only press the centre.
- **Stretches unevenly.**
  → You moved too fast in one direction. Rotate the dough more.

## Knuckle technique (advanced)

Once you're comfortable with the hand stretch, try this for a more
professional result:

1. Drape the disc over the back of both fists.
2. Slowly rotate your fists in opposite directions while drawing them
   apart — the dough naturally stretches between them.
3. Use gravity. The dough's own weight pulls it longer.
4. Watch the dough, not your hands. Stop when the middle is the right
   thickness.

This is the technique you see in pizzeria videos. It takes practice and
will tear the first 3-5 times.

## A few specific style notes

- **Neapolitan**: stretch to ~28-30 cm. Leave a 2 cm rim untouched. The
  centre should be quite thin (2-3 mm).
- **NY**: stretch to 14-15 inches at home (a full 18 won't fit most
  ovens). Centre 3-4 mm. Rim 1 cm.
- **Bar pies**: stretch to 8-10 inches. Same proportions as NY but
  smaller.
- **Roman tonda**: roll with a rolling pin (the only style that uses one
  legitimately). Aim for 1.5-2 mm thickness.
- **Pan styles**: not stretching, pressing. See above.

## The peel transfer

Once the dough is stretched, you have a clock. Transfer to a
semolina-dusted peel **immediately**, top quickly (under 60 seconds), and
launch. The longer the dough sits on the peel, the more moisture it
absorbs from the peel and the harder it becomes to launch.

A safety net: assemble the entire pizza on **parchment paper** placed
on the peel. Launch with the parchment under the pizza. After 2 minutes
in the oven, the dough sets — slide the parchment out at that point.

This costs you maybe 5% of bottom crispiness but it eliminates the
biggest pizza-night frustration: the dreaded "the pizza won't budge off
the peel" moment that ends with a balled-up mess.

## A meta-rule

Stretching is a skill that improves linearly with practice. Pizza #5
will be much better than pizza #1 in this dimension alone. Don't expect
perfection on attempt one. Most home pizzas get visibly better between
attempts 3 and 8 just from stretching technique improvement.
