# Grilled pizza and outdoor pizza ovens

Two related-but-different worlds: cooking pizza **on** a grill (using the
grill as oven) and cooking pizza **in** a purpose-built outdoor pizza oven.
Each is its own technique.

## Grilled pizza — pizza directly on the grates

A genuine American invention, popularised by Al Forno restaurant in
Providence, RI in the 1980s. The technique is unusual but the result is
unique: a smoky, charred, slightly chewy crust unlike anything you can
get in an oven.

### How it works

The dough is cooked **directly on the grill grates** (not on a stone).
You cook the bottom first, flip the dough, then top the cooked side and
finish.

### The dough

A NY or grandma-style dough works. Lower hydration is easier (60-65%) —
high hydration sticks to the grates.

### The method

1. Heat the grill to **maximum** (gas or charcoal). Gas: all burners on
   high, lid closed, 10-15 min preheat. Charcoal: a thick layer of hot
   coals.
2. **Brush grates clean and oil them well.** A wet paper towel held with
   tongs, soaked in vegetable oil, dragged across hot grates. Critical
   step — sticking ruins everything.
3. Stretch a dough ball to a small (10-12 inch) round on a peel dusted
   with semolina. Brush the top with olive oil.
4. **Carefully drop the dough onto the grates, oiled side down.**
5. Close the lid. After 1-2 minutes, the bottom is charred and the top
   has bubbled. Open the lid.
6. **Use tongs and a spatula to flip the dough.** Cooked side is now up.
7. **Quickly top the cooked side** with sauce, cheese, toppings. Work
   fast — the bottom is cooking.
8. Close the lid for 2-3 more minutes. The cheese melts; the bottom
   crisps further.
9. Remove. Add finishing touches (basil, oil, herbs).

### Why it tastes special

- The direct grate contact creates char marks the way a steakhouse does.
- The smoke from the grill (especially charcoal) flavours the dough.
- The flip means both sides get direct heat treatment — neither side is
  "the soggy side."

### Common grilled pizza mistakes

- **Grates not hot enough.** The dough will stick instead of charring.
- **Grates not oiled.** Same — sticking.
- **Toppings on first** (the wet-side-down problem). Always flip first,
  then top.
- **Over-topping** (too much weight on a cooking dough). Light hand.
- **Walking away.** This goes fast. Stay at the grill.

## Outdoor pizza ovens

A separate world: portable propane or wood-fired ovens that reach
800-1000°F, designed specifically for pizza. The breakthrough product
category that's transformed home pizza in the 2020s.

### The major brands

- **Ooni**: the category leader. Karu 12 (wood/propane), Koda 12 (propane
  only), Karu 16 (larger). The Karu 12 is the home pizza oven for most
  people: ~$350, makes excellent 12-inch pizzas, propane convenience.
- **Gozney**: Roccbox (~$500) and Dome (~$2000). Heavier-built, better
  insulated, more "permanent installation" feel.
- **Solo Stove**: Pi (propane). Decent budget option.
- **Pizza Party** (Italian): wood-fired only, smaller.

### What changes when you have one

The fundamental change is **temperature**. Your steel-in-a-home-oven was
maxed at 550°F. An outdoor oven hits 900°F. That difference is:

- **True Neapolitan in 60-90 seconds** — finally achievable at home.
- Much faster bake times across all styles.
- More dramatic leopard char and cornicione puff.

### Technique — different from indoors

#### Use the right dough

Outdoor ovens dry doughs out faster. Use **higher hydration** doughs
(67-70% for NY-adjacent, 65% for Neapolitan).

#### Bake fast

A 12-inch Neapolitan in an Ooni at 900°F is done in 60-90 seconds. You
have to:

- **Pre-position toppings nearby** before launching.
- **Rotate every 15-20 seconds** with a turning peel — the side facing
  the flame cooks much faster than the back.
- **Don't walk away.** A pizza burns in 30 seconds at 900°F.

#### Manage cold-spot rotation

Most outdoor ovens have a flame on one side. The opposite side of the
oven floor is cooler. Rotate the pizza every 15-20 seconds to expose
each section to the heat.

#### Preheat thoroughly

The temperature gauge often reads "hot enough" before the stone is fully
saturated. Wait an extra 5-10 minutes after the gauge says you're at
temperature. The stone needs to fully heat through.

#### Use a temperature gun

An infrared thermometer ($15) helps you read the actual stone
temperature. Target 800-900°F for Neapolitan, 700°F for NY-adapted, 600°F
for a pan-style.

### What outdoor ovens are not as good for

- **Detroit / Sicilian / Roman al taglio**: these are pan pizzas. They
  fit, but the dome heat will burn the top before the centre cooks.
  Standard home oven is better.
- **Chicago deep-dish**: definitely use a home oven (longer, lower-temp
  bake).
- **Large pizzas**: most home outdoor ovens fit 12-13 inch pies. The 16-inch
  Ooni Karu 16 can do larger. Anything bigger needs a built-in oven.

The pattern: outdoor pizza ovens excel at small, high-heat pies
(Neapolitan, bar pies, single-eater NY). They're complementary to your
home oven, not a replacement.

### Fuel choice — gas vs wood

- **Gas** (propane): consistent, fast preheat (15-20 min), no smoke
  flavour. Easy to use. The right pick for most people.
- **Wood**: requires sourcing kiln-dried hardwood pizza chunks (oak,
  maple, fruit woods). Adds smoke flavour. Harder to control temperature.
  Beautiful when it works.
- **Wood + gas** (Karu): hybrid lets you use gas for convenience and wood
  for special occasions.

Start with gas. Add wood capability later if you discover you want it.

### Outdoor oven safety

- **Outdoors only.** Never use a propane pizza oven indoors — CO risk.
- **Stable surface.** Put it on a heat-proof surface (concrete patio,
  metal pizza-oven table). Not directly on a wood deck.
- **Cool-down time**: most outdoor ovens stay hot for 30-60 minutes after
  shutoff. Keep kids and pets away.
- **Don't pour water on a hot stone**. The stone can crack from thermal
  shock.

## Cast-iron skillet pizza on the grill

A hybrid technique: use a cast-iron skillet on a hot grill as a makeshift
pizza oven.

1. Preheat the grill to 500°F+ with a cast-iron skillet inside (lid
   closed).
2. Stretch dough into a 10-inch round.
3. Open the grill, drop the dough into the hot skillet.
4. Top quickly.
5. Close the grill lid. Bake 7-9 minutes.

The bottom crisps in the cast iron; the closed lid acts as a dome to
brown the cheese. Result: a pretty respectable approximation of pizzeria
pizza, made on a regular gas grill.

## Choosing between methods

For your **first** outdoor-cooked pizza: try a cast-iron pizza on the
grill. Costs $0 if you already have a skillet and a grill.

For **occasional** outdoor pizza: grilled pizza directly on the grates.
Unique flavour, no special equipment.

For **regular** pizza-as-a-thing-you-do: get an Ooni Koda 12 or
equivalent. The leap in quality is real.
