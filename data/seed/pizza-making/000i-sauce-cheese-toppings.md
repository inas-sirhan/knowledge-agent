# Sauce, cheese, toppings — the upper-layer choices that make or break a pie

The dough is the foundation. Everything on top is what most people remember.
This document goes through tomato sauce, cheese, and toppings with enough
depth that you can hold an opinion on every choice.

## Sauce

### Cooked vs raw

The fundamental sauce decision:

- **Raw** (Neapolitan, some Roman): crushed quality tomatoes + salt. The
  oven cooks it during baking (typically 60-90 seconds at 900°F).
- **Cooked** (NY, Detroit, Sicilian, Chicago): tomatoes are simmered with
  aromatics for 15-30 minutes, **reduced**, cooled, then used. The bake
  time at 500-650°F isn't enough to reduce raw sauce, and a wet sauce ruins
  the crust.

Get this match right: raw sauce + high heat (Neapolitan), cooked sauce +
moderate heat (everything else). The opposite combinations give you either
burnt or watery sauce.

### Tomato choice

Tomatoes vary wildly in quality. Worth caring about:

- **San Marzano DOP**: the canonical pizza tomato. From Campania, near
  Naples. Sweet, low-acid, low-seed. Look for the "DOP" stamp and the
  Consorzio numbering on the can.
- **Bianco DiNapoli**: California-grown organic, exceptional quality, often
  better than mass-market San Marzano. Industry favourite among American
  pizzerias.
- **Cento San Marzano**: widely available, decent quality.
- **Generic whole peeled tomatoes**: usually fine if no premium option.
  Crush by hand and you'll be 90% of the way there.

**Crushed tomato** is convenient but lower quality on average. Whole peeled,
hand-crushed, gives you control over chunkiness.

**Tomato paste** is sometimes added (1-2 tbsp per can) to boost umami and
thicken. Don't overdo it — tomato paste is sweeter and more concentrated.

### A canonical NY sauce recipe

```
1 × 28 oz (800 g) whole peeled tomatoes
2 cloves garlic, grated or minced
1 tsp dried oregano
1 tsp fine sea salt
1 tsp sugar
2 tbsp olive oil
A small pinch of red pepper flakes (optional)
```

1. Heat olive oil over medium-low. Add garlic, cook ~1 min until fragrant.
   Don't brown.
2. Add hand-crushed tomatoes (or pulse in a blender for a smoother sauce —
   never run blender too long or it whips air in).
3. Add oregano, salt, sugar, pepper flakes.
4. Simmer uncovered 15-25 min, stirring occasionally, until reduced to a
   thick "spoonable, not pourable" consistency.
5. Cool completely before using. Refrigerate up to a week, or freeze in
   ice-cube trays.

### A canonical Neapolitan sauce

```
1 can quality whole peeled tomatoes (or fresh in summer)
1 tsp fine sea salt
```

1. Hand-crush the tomatoes in a bowl.
2. Add salt.
3. Done. Use immediately or refrigerate.

That's it. The oven does the rest.

## Cheese

### Low-moisture mozzarella

The American pizzeria standard, used in NY, Detroit, Sicilian, grandma,
bar pies, and most American styles. **Critical: buy in block form and
shred yourself.** Pre-shredded mozzarella is coated with cellulose or
potato starch to prevent caking; these prevent it from melting smoothly.

Good brands: Galbani Whole Milk, Polly-O Whole Milk, Saputo, Grande.

Use ~150-200 g per 12-inch pie (more on Detroit due to frico edges).

### Fresh mozzarella (fior di latte)

Used on Neapolitan and some bianca / margherita pies. Made from cow's milk
(buffalo if you can get it — bufala mozzarella is even richer).

**The problem with fresh mozzarella**: it releases water as it heats. If
you put a sopping wet ball straight on a pizza, you'll get a soggy mess.
Solutions:

1. Drain it 20-30 minutes before use, in a colander, lightly salted.
2. Slice it 1-2 hours ahead and let it air-dry on paper towels.
3. Tear into pieces rather than slicing — small pieces release water faster
   and reach equilibrium.

### Buffalo mozzarella

Made from water buffalo milk, more expensive, richer flavour, more
moisture. Used on classic Neapolitan margherita. Drain even harder than
cow's-milk fresh mozz.

### Aged provolone

A nice addition to Detroit and Sicilian — adds a slight tangy sharpness.
Use a small amount alongside mozz (10-15% of total cheese).

### Pecorino Romano

A sharp, salty grating cheese (sheep's milk). Used on Roman al taglio,
Roman tonda, NY (as a finishing dust), New Haven (essential). Don't bake
it long — it burns. Apply after the bake or in the last minute.

### Parmesan (Parmigiano Reggiano)

Similar to Pecorino but milder, made from cow's milk. Either is fine on
most pies.

### Brick cheese

Detroit-style essential. Wisconsin brick. If unavailable, blend
low-moisture mozz with mild cheddar (50/50) for a workable substitute.

### Ricotta

White pizza topping. Use fresh or whole-milk ricotta. Drain if very wet.
Often blended with grated parmesan and seasoned with salt and pepper before
applying. Dot in spoonfuls (don't spread continuously).

### Provel

St. Louis specialty. Hard to find elsewhere; substitute is provolone +
American cheese.

## Toppings — the rules

Toppings interact with each other in the oven. Pile them on indiscriminately
and you get a wet mess. A few principles:

### Moisture management

The hardest problem in pizza topping. Wet toppings (raw mushrooms, wet
sausage, fresh tomato, wet onions) release water during baking. This water
either evaporates (steaming your crust soggy) or pools.

Mitigations:

- **Pre-cook wet toppings.** Sauté mushrooms with salt until they've
  released their water and re-absorbed it. Cook sausage and drain fat
  before adding to pizza. Soften onions and peppers in a pan first.
- **Pat dry** pre-cooked toppings before adding to pizza.
- **Salt raw vegetables 20 min before**, then squeeze dry (especially
  zucchini and eggplant).
- **Use a thicker, drier sauce** if you have wet toppings.
- **Top sparingly.** A common amateur mistake is heaping too many toppings.

### Layering order — general

1. Sauce (thin, even, with ~1 cm border for crust).
2. Cheese.
3. "Wet" toppings under the cheese: pepperoni, raw sausage, etc.
4. "Dry" toppings on top of the cheese: onions, peppers, olives,
   anchovies, basil added after bake.
5. Hard cheese (pecorino, parmesan) applied lightly after or in last
   minute.

Notable exceptions: Detroit (cheese first, sauce on top after baking),
Chicago deep-dish (cheese on bottom), Trenton tomato pie (cheese first,
sauce on top).

### Pepperoni specifically

Two classes:

- **Large-cut deli pepperoni** (the standard supermarket kind, ~4-5 cm
  diameter). Stays flat in the oven, milder flavour. Fine but unexciting.
- **Small-cup pepperoni** (~3 cm diameter, with a natural casing or
  formulated to "cup and char"). The premium pizzeria choice. Brands:
  Vermont Smoke and Cure, Hormel Rosa Grande, Battistoni, Ezzo. These
  curl into bowls in the oven, filling with rendered fat, then char.

Vermont Smoke and Cure and Ezzo are widely available online; both produce
classic cup-and-char in any home oven.

### Sausage

- **Italian sausage**, sweet (with fennel) or hot. Remove from casing if
  bought as links. Break into 1 cm clumps.
- Apply **raw** to the pizza — the bake will cook it through.
- For very high-heat ovens (Neapolitan), pre-cook briefly so it's not raw
  in 90 seconds.

### Mushrooms

- **Slice thin** (3-4 mm), don't dice.
- For NY-style: raw, sliced thin, applied on top of cheese — they'll cook
  through in 6 minutes.
- For longer bakes or fresh mozz: pre-sauté with salt and a drop of oil
  to drive off moisture.

### Onions, peppers

- Pre-soften in a pan, OR slice very thin and let them cook in the pizza.
- Caramelised onions are spectacular — make them in advance, store in a
  jar, dollop onto white pizzas.

### Basil

- **Always after the bake.** Hot oven destroys basil's flavour.
- Tear (don't slice — the cut releases enzymes that brown the leaf).
- Drizzle with olive oil after.

### Garlic

- Fresh garlic + 500°F oven = burnt bitter garlic.
- Use **roasted garlic**, garlic confit, or garlic oil instead — these are
  cooked already and won't burn.
- Or apply **finely minced raw garlic mixed into olive oil** — it'll
  cook gently in the oven without burning.

### Olive oil drizzle

Apply **after** the bake. Use extra-virgin (good stuff). It carries flavour
the heat would have destroyed.

### Less-common but excellent toppings

- **Caramelised onions** on white pizza.
- **Hot honey** drizzled after bake on pepperoni pizza (a modern classic).
- **Anchovies** under cheese — they dissolve into salty umami.
- **Mortadella + pistachio cream + lemon zest** added after bake (a Roman
  al taglio classic).
- **Burrata** added after bake (don't bake it — it loses its creamy
  interior).
- **Prosciutto** added after bake.
- **Arugula tossed with lemon and olive oil** piled on after bake.
- **Egg cracked on top of pizza before the last 3 minutes of bake** (cooks
  to a runny yolk).

## A few rules of thumb

- **Less is more.** Three good toppings beat eight mediocre ones.
- **Salt your toppings.** Underseasoned toppings on great dough = a
  forgettable pizza.
- **Consider colours.** Three different colours of toppings (red sauce,
  white cheese, green basil) reads as a complete pizza visually.
- **Strong-flavoured cheeses go in small amounts** — a blanket of
  Pecorino will ruin the pie. A finishing dust will elevate it.

## Composition reference

For a 12-inch (30 cm) pizza:

- Sauce: ~80-100 g
- Cheese: ~150-180 g (Neapolitan: 80-100 g)
- Each topping: ~50-80 g (less for strong flavours like pepperoni or
  anchovy; more for vegetables that lose volume).

A pizza weighing more than 600 g pre-bake is probably over-topped.
