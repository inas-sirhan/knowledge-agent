# Sicilian and Roman styles — the rectangular pizzas

Both Sicilian and Roman pizzas are rectangular, baked in a pan, and cut into
squares. Both are thicker than their round cousins. But they're very
different pies — different dough chemistry, different role in a meal, different
toppings logic. Worth treating them as distinct traditions.

## Sicilian — "sfincione" and its American descendant

In Sicily itself, "sfincione" is the local pizza-like dish: a thick, focaccia-
style bread topped with onions, anchovies, oregano, breadcrumbs, and a small
amount of caciocavallo or tuma cheese (not mozzarella). It's the everyday
pizza-eaten-by-Sicilians.

When Sicilian immigrants moved to America (especially the Brooklyn area in
the early 20th century), they adapted it: kept the thick focaccia base, but
added the American conventions — mozzarella, more tomato sauce, more
toppings. The result is what most American pizzerias call "Sicilian" — a
thick square slice, usually 5 cm tall, with a focaccia-like interior and a
crisp bottom from being baked in an oiled pan.

### What it looks like

- Rectangular, baked in a sheet pan or **teglia**.
- Cut into squares (usually 8–12 per pan).
- Crust ~3–4 cm tall, with a slight crispness on the bottom and a soft,
  open crumb.
- Generously topped: tomato sauce, melted mozzarella, sometimes additional
  toppings.

### Dough (American Sicilian)

```
500 g  bread flour
350 g  water (70% hydration)
12  g  salt
 4  g  instant yeast
 25 g  olive oil
```

1. Mix wet, add dry, knead 8 min.
2. Bulk ferment 60 min.
3. **Cold ferment 24–48 hours** in the fridge for flavour.
4. Press dough into a generously oiled 9×13 (or 13×18) sheet pan.
5. Proof at room temp 60–90 min until the dough fills the pan.
6. Top with sauce-then-cheese (sauce under cheese here, unlike Detroit).
7. Bake at 500°F / 260°C, lower-middle rack, 18–22 min.

### Toppings

- A heavy hand with **tomato sauce** — Sicilian is tomato-forward.
- **Low-moisture mozzarella**, edge to edge but not piled.
- Optional: pepperoni, sausage, onions, anchovies, dried oregano.
- A drizzle of olive oil after the bake.

The big stylistic difference from Detroit: Sicilian bakes with the **sauce
under the cheese, layered conventionally**. It is a deeper, more cohesive
slice — closer to a focaccia-pizza fusion than to Detroit's frico-edged
specialty.

## Roman al taglio — the long pizza sold by weight

This is what Romans eat for lunch. **Pizza al taglio** ("by the cut") is a
long, low rectangle of pizza — often 1+ metre long — sold by weight in shops
called *pizzicheria al taglio*. You point at what you want, the shopkeeper
cuts a piece with scissors, weighs it, and hands it over wrapped in paper.

### Why it matters as a home-cook style

It is one of the easiest pizzas to make at home. You don't have to stretch
individual rounds. The dough is sticky and high-hydration, and you spread it
in a pan with your fingers. The pan is the structure. And because it bakes
slower than Neapolitan, you have time to get it right.

### What it looks like

- A long rectangular pan pizza (use a half-sheet pan at home: ~45×33 cm).
- Crust **2.5–3 cm tall**, with a **shatteringly crisp** bottom and a
  cavernous, alveolar interior.
- Toppings vary by section — one pan might have three different toppings
  in three sections.

### Dough — the highest-hydration pizza in this KB

```
500 g  strong bread flour (or 50/50 bread + 00)
400 g  water (80% hydration)
12  g  salt
  2  g  instant yeast
 25 g  olive oil
```

1. Combine. Mix to shaggy dough. Rest 20 min.
2. Do 4 stretch-and-folds at 30-min intervals (no kneading — high hydration
   develops gluten via folds, not by kneading).
3. Cold ferment **48–72 hours**. This is non-negotiable for the open crumb.
4. The day of baking: dust your counter heavily with semolina and flour.
   Tip dough out. Stretch gently into a rough rectangle.
5. Place onto a heavily oiled half-sheet pan. Use your fingertips to dimple
   it to fit the pan.
6. Proof at room temp 60–90 min.
7. Bake at maximum oven temp (550°F / 290°C). For best results, **par-bake
   the dough plain for 5 minutes**, then add toppings and finish another
   10–12 min. Total bake: ~17 min.

### Toppings — the al taglio repertoire

The defining trait is modularity. You can top one section with classic
margherita, another with potato-and-rosemary (a Roman classic), another
with mortadella-and-pistachio (more modern). Toppings can be applied
**after** the bake for delicate ingredients (mortadella, prosciutto crudo,
arugula, burrata).

Some canonical toppings:

- **Margherita section**: thin tomato base, mozz, basil after bake.
- **Potato-rosemary**: paper-thin potato slices, olive oil, fresh rosemary,
  sea salt. No sauce, no cheese.
- **Mortadella + pistachio cream** (added after bake): mozz + pistachio
  cream baked into the dough; mortadella slices laid on after.
- **Funghi (mushroom)**: sautéed mushrooms, mozz, thyme.

## Roman tonda — the audibly-crunchy round

Don't confuse al taglio with **tonda al mattarello** — the classic round
Roman pizza, very thin (rolled with a rolling pin, not stretched), with a
cracker-crisp texture and no cornicione. The dough is lower-hydration
(55–60%) than al taglio and bakes very quickly at high heat.

It's the opposite of Neapolitan: dry, audible crunch, dense base. Less
forgiving than al taglio at home. Most home cooks don't bother — if you want
thin, NY or al taglio give better results.

## Choosing between them

| You want | Pick |
|---|---|
| Foccacia-thick, generous tomato, low effort | American Sicilian |
| The most home-oven-forgiving thick pizza | Roman al taglio |
| To impress guests with different toppings per slice | Roman al taglio |
| A cracker-thin crisp Italian-style | Roman tonda (or just go NY) |

## Common rectangular-pizza mistakes

- **Not letting the dough proof in the pan.** Sicilian and al taglio both
  rise *in* the pan to fill it. Skip this and you get a dense flat slab.
- **Under-oiling the pan.** Olive oil in the pan is what fries the bottom
  into crispness. Be generous (3 tbsp+ for a half-sheet).
- **Pre-baking too long for al taglio.** 5 min plain par-bake is enough.
  Longer, and the dough dries out before toppings can soak in.
- **Too much sauce on Sicilian.** The thick crust will go soggy under a wet
  sauce. Reduce your sauce first.

## Why these pizzas are forgiving

The rectangular pan does the hard structural work. You don't have to launch
a stretched round onto a steel. You don't have to balance a flop. The pan
holds the shape, the high hydration creates the open interior, and the oil
creates the crispy bottom. For someone who wants to step up from store-
bought without buying outdoor pizza ovens, Roman al taglio and American
Sicilian are the right entry points.
