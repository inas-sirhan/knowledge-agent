# Dough fundamentals — the four ingredients and what they do

Every pizza dough in this knowledge base is some combination of flour,
water, salt, and yeast (with occasional additions of oil, sugar, or
malt). Understanding what each ingredient does, and what the ratios mean,
is what lets you adapt any recipe to your equipment and to substitute
intelligently.

This document is the underlying chemistry. The style-specific docs are the
applications.

## Baker's percentage — read this first

Pizza recipes are written in **baker's percentage** (also called "baker's
math"). The flour is always 100%, and every other ingredient is expressed
as a percentage **of the flour weight by mass**. So a recipe like:

- Flour 100%
- Water 65%
- Salt 2.5%
- Yeast 0.3%
- Oil 2%

means: for every 1000 g of flour, you use 650 g water, 25 g salt, 3 g
yeast, 20 g oil. Total dough = 1698 g. If you need 4 dough balls of
260 g each, scale: target 1040 g of dough. Divide every number by
1698/1040 ≈ 1.63. So: 614 g flour, 399 g water, etc.

Once you read recipes this way, you can:

- Compare styles directly ("Neapolitan is 65% hydration, Roman al taglio is
  80% — that's why the latter is so much wetter")
- Scale recipes up and down without arithmetic puzzles
- Notice when something's off ("3% salt? That's the same as the ocean")

## The four core ingredients

### Flour

Flour provides starch (the bulk) and gluten-forming proteins (glutenin +
gliadin) that, once hydrated, form the elastic network that traps gas. The
**protein percentage** of a flour determines how strong the dough's
structure will be.

- **All-purpose (AP) flour**: ~10–11% protein. Soft, tender doughs.
  Good for grandma, Chicago thin tavern. Doesn't have the strength for a
  long cold ferment or a thin stretch.
- **Bread flour**: ~12–13% protein. The workhorse for NY, Detroit,
  Sicilian. Tolerates a long cold ferment and stretches well.
- **High-gluten flour** (King Arthur Sir Galahad / All Trumps): ~13–14%
  protein. The traditional NY pizzeria flour. Most home cooks won't need
  more than bread flour.
- **Italian "00" flour**: a **grind classification**, not a protein
  level. "00" is the finest grind. Pizza-specific 00 flours (Caputo
  Pizzeria, Polselli Classica) come in at 11–13% protein. The fine grind
  gives a tender Neapolitan crumb; the high protein supports long ferments.
- **Semolina**: coarsely ground durum wheat. Used in some Sicilian doughs
  for texture, and as a dusting flour because it doesn't burn on a hot
  stone.

You can't substitute AP for high-gluten in a NY recipe and expect the same
results — the structure will collapse under stretching and toppings. You
can substitute bread flour for 00 in a Neapolitan and get a respectable
result; the crumb will be slightly tougher.

### Water

Water hydrates the flour, activates the gluten network, and provides the
medium for yeast to do its work. The **hydration percentage** is one of
the two or three most important numbers in a recipe.

- **55–60%**: very dry doughs — Roman tonda, Chicago tavern. Crackery,
  dense.
- **60–65%**: NY, classic Neapolitan. Standard range.
- **65–70%**: Detroit, grandma — more open crumb, slightly harder to
  handle.
- **70–80%**: Roman al taglio, Sicilian, focaccia-adjacent — wet and
  sticky, gives a very open, alveolar interior. Hard to knead conventionally;
  develop gluten via stretch-and-fold instead.

Higher hydration = more open crumb + harder to handle. There's no virtue
in pushing hydration past what the style calls for.

Water quality usually doesn't matter for home cooks. Tap water is fine
unless your water is heavily chlorinated (yeast-killing); in that case let
the water sit out overnight.

### Salt

Salt does three things:

1. Adds flavour.
2. Strengthens the gluten network.
3. Slows yeast activity, which is essential for long ferments.

Pizza doughs typically use 2.5–3% salt. Less than 2% and the dough tastes
flat and rises too fast. More than 3.5% and the yeast struggles.

Use **fine sea salt** by weight. If you're using kosher salt, weigh it —
volume measurements are unreliable across kosher brands.

**Don't dissolve yeast directly into salt water.** Salt-killed yeast won't
proof. Either dissolve the salt in the water first and add yeast later, or
mix the salt into the flour and add the wet-yeasted water separately.

### Yeast

Yeast eats sugars, produces CO2 (the rise) and alcohol/acids (the flavour),
and dies off in the oven.

- **Instant dry yeast (IDY)**: the most common modern choice. Add directly
  to dough. ~3-5% used at the start of any recipe.
- **Active dry yeast (ADY)**: needs to be proofed in warm water before use.
  Use ~25% more by weight than IDY.
- **Fresh yeast (cake yeast)**: traditional for Neapolitan. Use ~3× the
  weight of IDY. Refrigerate; lasts ~2 weeks.
- **Sourdough starter**: a wild-yeast + lactobacillus culture. Replaces
  commercial yeast at ~10–20% of flour weight. Adds tang and longer
  fermentation; ferment ~6–12 hours longer.

The **amount** of yeast matters more than which kind:

- Higher yeast % + shorter ferment = faster, less flavour.
- Lower yeast % + longer ferment = better flavour, more digestible dough
  (because the cold ferment lets the yeast and bacteria do more
  fermentation, breaking down complex starches).

Most quality pizza recipes use **0.1–0.5% instant yeast** with a 24–72 hour
cold ferment.

## The optional ingredients

### Oil

- Adds flavour (use a quality olive oil if it's going in).
- Tenderises the crumb (interferes with gluten).
- Aids browning at lower oven temperatures.
- Typical: 2–5% for NY, Detroit, Sicilian. Zero for Neapolitan and
  Roman al taglio.

### Sugar (or malt)

- Feeds the yeast, accelerating rise.
- Caramelises in the oven, helping browning — essential for NY pizza
  baked at 600°F (lower than Neapolitan's 900°F).
- Typical: 1–3% for NY. Zero for Neapolitan (the 900°F heat does the
  browning).
- **Diastatic malt powder** is a more controlled alternative — it
  provides enzymes that break starch into sugars gradually during
  fermentation. Used by serious pizzerias.

## The mixing and gluten development

There are three ways to develop gluten:

1. **Kneading** (mechanical). The traditional approach. Knead by hand or
   mixer until the dough passes the **windowpane test** — stretch a small
   piece thin and it should be translucent without tearing.
2. **Stretch-and-fold** (time-based). Better for high-hydration doughs
   that are too sticky to knead. Every 30 minutes, gently pull one edge
   of the dough up and fold over the centre. Rotate the bowl. Repeat 3-4
   times.
3. **Autolyse + brief knead**. Mix only flour and water; rest 20-60
   minutes; then add salt and yeast, knead briefly. The rest period
   allows the flour to fully hydrate and gluten to start forming on its
   own.

You can combine these. A canonical NY dough mixes briefly, autolyses 20
minutes, then mixer-kneads 8 minutes. A Roman al taglio mixes briefly,
then does 4 stretch-and-folds over 2 hours.

## Bulk fermentation vs. ball fermentation

Most pizza doughs ferment in two stages:

1. **Bulk fermentation**: the full mass of dough rises in a bowl. Usually
   1–2 hours at room temperature.
2. **Ball fermentation**: the dough is divided into individual portions
   and rises again. Usually another 4–24 hours, often in the fridge.

The split allows for **portioning** at the right moment (after gluten is
formed but before the dough is at peak rise) and for individual doughs
to ferment in shape for easier stretching.

## Cold ferment — the secret of great pizza

A long cold ferment (24–72 hours in the fridge, at 38°F / 3°C) is the
single biggest flavour upgrade you can make to your pizza. During the
slow ferment:

- Enzymes break down complex starches into simple sugars (sweeter, more
  flavourful crust).
- Lactobacilli produce mild acids (depth of flavour, slight tang).
- Gluten relaxes and stretches more easily.
- Dough develops digestibility (yeast and bacteria pre-digest some
  starches).

For NY, Detroit, Sicilian, and al taglio doughs: **always cold-ferment
at least 24 hours, ideally 48–72.** The difference between a same-day NY
dough and a 48-hour NY dough is enormous.

For Neapolitan: 24-hour cold ferment is standard. Longer than 48 hours
risks over-fermentation given the high temperature in 00 flour.

## What can go wrong

- **Salt directly on yeast** → dead yeast → no rise.
- **Water too warm** (>110°F / 43°C) → dead yeast.
- **Water too cold** (<60°F / 15°C) → slow start, hard to recover in a
  short ferment.
- **Over-fermented** → dough deflated, sticky, smells alcoholic. Catch
  it at room temp; if cold-fermented, simply use earlier next time.
- **Under-fermented** → dough is dense, tight, doesn't stretch. Let it
  rest longer.
- **Tough, tearing dough** → over-kneaded or low hydration. Reduce
  kneading time or raise hydration 2%.

## A mental model for any new pizza recipe

When you read a new recipe, ask:

1. What's the **flour** — protein level?
2. What's the **hydration**?
3. How **much yeast** and how long is the **ferment**?
4. **Cold or warm** ferment?
5. Any **oil/sugar**, and how much?

Those five answers tell you most of what the recipe will produce. A recipe
calling for AP flour at 60% hydration with 2% yeast and a 2-hour warm
ferment is "weeknight pizza" tier — fast, simple, not deep. A recipe
calling for bread flour at 65% with 0.3% yeast and 72-hour cold ferment is
"pizzeria-quality at home" tier — slower, more rewarding, much better.

Choose the recipe that matches your time and ambition, not the other way
around.
