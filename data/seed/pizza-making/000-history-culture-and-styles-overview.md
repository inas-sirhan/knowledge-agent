# Pizza — a working history and the major styles, compared

Pizza in its modern form was born in Naples in the 18th and 19th centuries.
Before that, flatbreads with toppings were ancient and global — Greek
plakous, focaccia in Liguria, Middle-Eastern manakish, Provençal pissaladière
— but the round, tomato-and-cheese-on-soft-leavened-dough pie we recognise
today was a Neapolitan invention, possible only after tomatoes (a New World
crop) had been accepted in the Italian South.

The pivotal moment was 1889, when chef Raffaele Esposito at Pizzeria Brandi
in Naples reportedly made a pie with tomato, mozzarella, and basil — the
colours of the new Italian flag — for visiting Queen Margherita di Savoia.
That pie became "pizza Margherita" and it remains the canonical statement of
the form: dough, tomato, mozzarella, basil, olive oil, salt.

## Naples → America → the world

Pizza left Naples with the great wave of Southern Italian emigration to the
United States between roughly 1880 and 1924. The first American pizzeria
generally credited is Lombardi's in Manhattan (1905). It was a working-class
food in immigrant neighbourhoods until the post-WWII era, when soldiers
returning from Italy and the rise of the suburb pushed pizza into the American
mainstream.

The crucial fact is that pizza in America didn't try to imitate Naples — it
mutated to fit American ovens, American flour, American expectations of
portion size, and American cheese. Each city's "style" is essentially the
solution that local pizzaiolos and immigrants worked out given the equipment
they had.

This is why the styles are not just "tastes." They are answers to very
different engineering problems.

## A guide to the major styles

The table below is a working mental model. Each style below has its own deep
dive in this knowledge base.

| Style | Where it's from | Crust character | Oven heat | Cooking time | Bottom-line vibe |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Neapolitan | Naples, Italy | Soft, blistered, charred, foldable | ~900°F / 482°C | 60–90 sec | "Wet" centre, leopard-spotted cornicione, sit-down with knife and fork |
| New York | NYC | Thin, crisp underneath, foldable in half lengthwise | 600–650°F / 315–340°C | 5–7 min | Big slices, dripping cheese, the classic American pizzeria pie |
| Detroit | Detroit | Thick, airy, oily cheese-crusted edge ("frico"), light interior | 500–550°F | 12–15 min | Rectangle, edges and corners are the prize, sauce often on top |
| Sicilian (US) | Brooklyn / Sicily-origin | Thick, focaccia-like, square slice | 500–550°F | 15–20 min | Tomato-forward, often without mozz, dense and chewy |
| Roman al taglio | Rome | Long, low rectangular shapes, sold by weight | 550°F+ | 12–20 min | High hydration, large crisp shards, modular toppings |
| Roman tonda | Rome | Round, very thin, cracker-crisp | 550°F+ | 4–6 min | Audibly crunchy, a thinner cousin of NY |
| Chicago deep-dish | Chicago | Tall buttery / cornmeal crust, cheese-then-sauce inversion | 425–450°F | 30–45 min | A casserole. Knife-and-fork required. |
| Chicago tavern (thin) | Chicago | Cracker-thin, cut into squares ("party cut") | 500°F | 8–10 min | Local favourite, very different from deep dish |
| Grandma | Long Island | Thin square pan, lightly fried bottom | 500–550°F | 12–15 min | Garlicky, fresh tomato, low-effort home reproducible |
| Bar pie | NJ / NYC bars | Thin, charred, served whole as a personal pie | 550°F+ | 6–8 min | Crispy, eaten with beer, single-eater |
| St. Louis | St. Louis | Cracker-thin, no yeast, Provel cheese, square cut | 550°F+ | 5–7 min | Polarising. Provel is processed and unique to the style. |
| Greek-American | New England | Pan-baked, oily-bottomed, regular dough | 500°F | 12–15 min | Lots of cheese, square or round, ubiquitous in NH/MA "house of pizza" shops |
| California | West Coast / Berkeley | Thin, often grilled, non-traditional toppings | 500–550°F | 8–10 min | Chez Panisse / Wolfgang Puck lineage — pizza as a vehicle for the season's produce |
| Detroit-style "Old World" pepperoni | Detroit | As above, cup-and-char pepperoni | 500–550°F | 12–15 min | A textural specialty within Detroit pizza |

The most useful question for a beginner is not "which style is best?" — they
are good at different things — but "which style works in **the oven I
already own**, with **the time I have**, at **the skill level I'm at**?"

## Cultural patterns worth knowing

- **Neapolitan is protected.** The Associazione Verace Pizza Napoletana
  (AVPN) certifies pizzerias that follow strict rules: type 00 flour, sea
  salt, fresh yeast (no instant), San Marzano tomatoes (PDO), fior di latte
  or buffalo mozzarella, wood-fired oven at 430–485°C, 60–90 second cook,
  diameter not more than 35 cm, cornicione 1–2 cm. Many pizzerias outside
  Italy claim Neapolitan style without certification — quality varies wildly.
- **NY pizza identity is generational.** Slice shops are a New York
  institution; the dollar-slice phenomenon (which peaked ~2008–2018) shaped a
  generation's idea of what pizza is. The rise of "elevated" pizzerias in the
  2010s (Roberta's, Lucali, Joe's, Una Pizza Napoletana) deliberately pushed
  back, often toward Neapolitan technique.
- **Detroit pizza is the fastest-growing American style.** The 2010s
  rediscovery of the Buddy's-style square pie by chefs Shawn Randazzo, Tony
  Gemignani, and others, plus the Ooni-driven home oven revolution, has put
  Detroit in restaurants worldwide.
- **Roman al taglio is the natural travel food.** Sold by weight, eaten
  standing, easy to top with whatever's fresh that day — it's how Romans
  actually eat pizza for lunch. The format is approachable for home cooks
  because you bake it as a tray instead of stretching individual rounds.
- **Chicago "deep dish vs tavern" is a local culture war.** Most Chicagoans
  eat the thin tavern-style ("party cut" in squares) far more often than
  deep dish, which is mostly for tourists. Both are legitimate Chicago
  pizza.

## How to think about choosing a style at home

1. **What's the maximum temperature your oven hits?** A standard home oven
   tops out around 250–290°C / 482–550°F. That rules out true Neapolitan,
   which needs ~480°C, unless you have an outdoor pizza oven. NY, Detroit,
   Sicilian, Roman al taglio, and grandma all work beautifully at 550°F.
2. **How much time do you have?** Neapolitan and grandma are same-day
   doughs (sometimes 6–24 hour ferment). Detroit and NY benefit from 24–72
   hour cold ferment for flavour. Roman al taglio is a 24+ hour cold ferment.
3. **How forgiving do you need the recipe to be?** For a first attempt:
   **Detroit, Sicilian, or grandma** are the easiest to nail — pan-based,
   forgiving of imperfect stretch, less stretching anxiety, harder to ruin.
   Round NY pizza requires confident stretching. Neapolitan requires extreme
   heat and very fast technique.
4. **What flavour do you want?** Chewy / soft → Neapolitan. Crispy under,
   pliable on top → NY. Buttery, cheese-laden, oily edges → Detroit. Bread-y
   focaccia base → Sicilian. Audible crunch → Roman tonda. Big edible
   sandwich-of-cheese → Chicago deep dish.

Spend an hour reading the deep-dive doc for the style you've chosen before
your first bake — same as you'd read a paper before running an experiment.
The styles aren't free-form vibes. Each one has dough chemistry, baking
temperature, and assembly order that work together as a system.
