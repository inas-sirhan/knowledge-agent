# Pizza on a budget — great pies for not much money

You can make excellent pizza on a tight budget. The premium equipment and
ingredients help at the margin, but the fundamentals are cheap and the
gap between a $5/serving home pizza and a $25/serving restaurant pizza
isn't as big as the price difference suggests.

## The cost of a great home pizza

A 12-inch home pizza with quality ingredients costs roughly:

- Dough (~300g of bread flour, water, salt, yeast, oil): $0.30
- Sauce (1/4 of a $3 can of San Marzano + seasonings): $1.00
- Low-moisture mozzarella (180g of $8/kg block): $1.50
- Pepperoni (50g of $20/kg quality cup-and-char): $1.00
- Olive oil + basil + salt: $0.30

Total: **~$4 per pizza**.

A take-out version of the same pizza costs $18-25. The savings per pizza is
$15-20. Even amortising equipment, you pay for a baking steel and pan in
2-3 home pizza nights.

## Cheapest reliable setup — under $100

You can make great pizza with very little equipment.

### Essentials ($60-90)

- **Baking steel**: $60 (1/4" hot-rolled steel plate from an industrial
  metal supplier; bigger brand-name steels are $80-110 but the metal is
  the same).
- **Sheet pan** ($10) — you may already have one.
- **Digital scale** ($15) — essential for consistency.
- **Wood spoon / mixing bowl** — already have these.

### Skip these on a budget

- Pizza peel ($15-30): use parchment paper. Assemble the pizza on parchment
  on the back of an upside-down sheet pan, launch by sliding the parchment
  onto the steel. Slide the parchment out after 2 minutes.
- Pizza wheel ($10): a sharp kitchen knife cuts pizza better than a wheel
  anyway.
- Outdoor pizza oven ($300+): the baking steel + home oven gets you 80%
  of the way there. Save up.
- Bench scraper ($8): a wide knife or a spatula works.
- Detroit-specific pan ($45): use a 9x13 cake pan.

## The cheapest dough ingredients

### Flour

- **Generic bread flour** from any supermarket: $3-4 for a 5-lb bag. Makes
  ~25 dough balls. About 15 cents per pie.
- **Italian 00 flour** ($8-12/kg) is a premium upgrade for Neapolitan. Skip
  it if you're not doing Neapolitan; bread flour is fine for everything
  else.

### Yeast

- **SAF Red Instant Yeast**, 500g brick: $5, lasts a year refrigerated.
  Roughly $0.001 per pie at 0.3% loadings. The cheapest ingredient.

### Salt

- Cheap fine sea salt: $2 a kilo. Same.

### Olive oil

- For the dough (2% of flour weight): generic extra-virgin olive oil at
  $10 for 750ml works fine in the dough; barely affects flavour because
  it's tiny in amount.
- For finishing drizzle: a slightly better olive oil ($15 for a 500ml of
  Greek or Italian estate-bottled) makes a noticeable difference.

## Sauce on a budget

The big choice: **whole peeled tomatoes vs San Marzano DOP**.

- **Cento San Marzano DOP**: $4-6 per 28-oz can. Yields enough sauce for
  4 pizzas. About $1-1.50/pie.
- **Generic whole peeled tomatoes** ($2/can): half the cost, 80% of the
  quality. The difference at home, in a hot oven, with all the other
  ingredients on top, is small.
- **Crushed tomato in a tin**: cheapest option, works fine. About
  $0.50/pie.

**Don't buy jarred pizza sauce.** It costs more than canned tomatoes, has
add-ins (sugar, preservatives), and is no better than what you make from
a can in 20 minutes.

## Cheese — the biggest spend

This is where budget shows. Cheese is the most expensive part of pizza
ingredient cost.

### Cheap and excellent

- **Galbani Whole Milk Low-Moisture Mozzarella block**: $8-10/kg at most
  supermarkets. Shred it yourself. Makes 5-6 pizzas. About $1.50-2/pie.

### Splurges that are worth it

- **Fresh mozzarella (cow's milk)** for Margherita: $6-8 for 250g.
  Enough for 2 pizzas. About $3-4/pie. Drains a lot of water — pat dry.

### Splurges that aren't worth it

- **Pre-shredded "Italian-style" cheese blends**: 50% more expensive than
  block low-moisture mozz, melts worse (due to cellulose anti-caking).
  Skip.
- **Artisanal Buffalo Mozzarella di Bufala Campana DOP**: $12-18 for 250g.
  Genuinely better but you pay a 4× premium over fresh cow's-milk mozz.
  Save it for special occasions.
- **Brick cheese for Detroit-style**: if not available locally, substitute
  50/50 low-moisture mozzarella + mild cheddar (similar behaviour, much
  cheaper).

## Pepperoni on a budget

- **Generic supermarket pepperoni** (Hormel Pepperoni, $4 for 200g): fine
  for most purposes. About $1/pie.
- **Premium cup-and-char pepperoni** (Vermont Smoke and Cure, Ezzo,
  Battistoni — $7-10 for 200g): significantly better cup-and-char
  performance and flavour depth. About $1.50-2/pie.

The premium pepperoni is the most cost-effective upgrade you can make.
Going from $4 to $8 on pepperoni doubles your flavour for an extra $1/pie.

## Toppings on a budget

The cheap and good list:

- **Fresh garlic**: 50 cents per head, lasts weeks. Make garlic confit
  ($1 of olive oil + 1 head = pizza topping for 2 weeks).
- **Yellow onions**: cheap, caramelise beautifully.
- **Dried oregano**: $2 for a bottle that lasts a year.
- **Mushrooms** (cremini): $4-5/lb. Sauté before topping.
- **Bell peppers**: $2-3 each, sliced thin.
- **Fresh basil**: $3 for a bunch. Grow it from a $1 seed packet — saves a
  fortune and tastes far better.

### Things to avoid for budget reasons

- **Buffalo mozzarella for everyday pizza** (use cow's-milk fresh mozz).
- **Imported San Daniele prosciutto** ($25/100g) — use a domestic
  prosciutto ($8-12) for everyday.
- **Truffle anything**.
- **Pine nuts** ($30+/lb) — substitute walnuts.

## A weekly budget pizza meal plan

If you make pizza once a week, total ingredients for 4 pizzas:

- Flour (1.5 kg): $1.50
- Yeast, salt, oil: $1.00
- 2 cans whole peeled tomatoes: $4.00
- Mozzarella block (1 kg): $9.00
- Pepperoni (200g): $5.00
- Onion + garlic + basil: $2.50

**Total for 4 pizzas: ~$23. About $5.75/pie.**

You're feeding 4-6 people for about $25, with restaurant-quality results.

## The biggest cost-saving move: bake more

The amortisation argument: a baking steel is $60. Across 100 pizzas (about
2 years of one-pizza-a-week), it's $0.60 per pizza. After that, it's free.

Same with the time investment of learning. The first 5 pizzas have a steep
learning curve. After that, dough takes 10 minutes of active time and
yields excellent pizzas. You're spending $4 of ingredients and 30 minutes
total to produce a $20 pizza, three times a week.

## When to splurge

Even on a tight budget, some upgrades are worth saving for:

1. **Excellent olive oil for finishing** ($15-25 bottle). Drizzles only —
   a bottle lasts months. Single biggest perceived-quality boost.
2. **Premium pepperoni** ($1-2 more per pie). Dramatic flavour difference.
3. **A baking steel** ($60 one-time). Transforms every pizza.
4. **An Ooni Koda 12** ($300 one-time). Worth it once you're committed.

## When NOT to splurge

- Pre-shredded "Italian" cheese blend.
- "Pizza yeast" (it's just instant yeast in a marketed package).
- Specialty pizza-themed equipment that isn't a steel or a pan.
- Imported water (some recipes are precious about this — your tap water
  is fine).
- Branded canned tomatoes with the word "pizza" on the label.

## The honest summary

You can make pizza nearly as good as a restaurant for $5/pie at home, with
$100 of equipment that lasts forever. The premium upgrades give you
incremental returns. The fundamentals — quality canned tomatoes, fresh
shredded cheese, a hot baking steel, and patience for cold fermentation —
are the things that matter. Everything else is optimisation.
